The History of Patek Philippe: The World's Most Prestigious Watchmaker

January 2025 • 15 min read

In the rarefied world of haute horlogerie, one name stands above all others: Patek Philippe. Founded in 1839, this Geneva-based manufacturer has spent nearly two centuries crafting what many consider the finest timepieces ever made. Their watches have graced the wrists of royalty, adorned the collections of billionaires, and set record after record at auction houses worldwide.

But what makes Patek Philippe so special? Why do collectors wait years—sometimes decades—for the privilege of purchasing certain models? And how did a small Swiss workshop become the undisputed pinnacle of watchmaking excellence?

"You never actually own a Patek Philippe. You merely look after it for the next generation."

This iconic advertising slogan, introduced in 1996, perfectly captures the brand's philosophy. A Patek Philippe isn't just a watch—it's a legacy, an heirloom, a piece of art meant to transcend time itself.

The Founding: A Partnership of Visionaries

The story begins in 1839 when two Polish immigrants, Antoni Patek and Franciszek Czapek, established a watchmaking business in Geneva. Patek, a former soldier who fought in the November Uprising against Russian rule, brought business acumen and aristocratic connections. Czapek contributed technical expertise.

However, the partnership was short-lived. In 1845, Patek met Jean Adrien Philippe, a brilliant French watchmaker who had invented the revolutionary keyless winding and hand-setting system—the same mechanism used in virtually every mechanical watch today. Recognizing Philippe's genius, Patek ended his partnership with Czapek and joined forces with Philippe in 1851.

The newly named Patek Philippe & Co. combined Patek's commercial talents with Philippe's technical innovations. It was a partnership that would change watchmaking forever.

A Timeline of Excellence

1839

Company founded in Geneva by Antoni Patek and Franciszek Czapek as "Patek, Czapek & Co."

1845

Antoni Patek meets Jean Adrien Philippe at the Paris Exhibition, impressed by his keyless winding mechanism.

1851

Patek Philippe & Co. officially established. Queen Victoria purchases a Patek Philippe pendant watch.

1868

Creation of the first Swiss wristwatch, made for Countess Koscowicz of Hungary.

1925

Introduction of the first perpetual calendar wristwatch.

1932

The Stern family acquires Patek Philippe, ensuring its independence to this day.

1933

Commission begins for the Henry Graves Supercomplication, the most complicated watch ever made at the time.

1976

Introduction of the Nautilus, designed by Gérald Genta, revolutionizing luxury sports watches.

1999

Caliber 89 unveiled for the 150th anniversary—the most complicated portable timepiece ever made with 33 complications.

2019

Grandmaster Chime sells for $31.19 million, the most expensive watch ever sold at auction.

Royal Connections

From its earliest days, Patek Philippe attracted the attention of royalty and nobility. Queen Victoria purchased a pendant watch in 1851, beginning a tradition of royal patronage that continues today. The brand's client list reads like a who's who of European aristocracy:

  • Queen Victoria — Purchased multiple timepieces for herself and Prince Albert
  • Pope Pius IX — Owned several Patek Philippe pocket watches
  • Prince Albert I of Monaco — Commissioned custom pieces
  • King Victor Emmanuel III of Italy — Avid collector
  • Countess Koscowicz of Hungary — Received the first Swiss wristwatch in 1868

This royal patronage wasn't merely symbolic—it validated Patek Philippe's position as the preeminent watchmaker of its era and established traditions of excellence that persist today.

The Stern Family Era

In 1932, during the Great Depression, the Stern family—themselves successful dial manufacturers—acquired Patek Philippe. This acquisition proved crucial to the brand's survival and future success. Unlike many luxury brands that have changed hands multiple times or been absorbed by conglomerates, Patek Philippe has remained family-owned for over 90 years.

The Stern family's stewardship has been characterized by:

  • Uncompromising quality — Never cutting corners, even during economic downturns
  • Limited production — Deliberately restricting output to maintain exclusivity and quality
  • Vertical integration — Manufacturing virtually every component in-house
  • Long-term thinking — Making decisions based on decades, not quarters

Today, Thierry Stern serves as President, representing the fourth generation of family leadership. Under his guidance, Patek Philippe has continued to innovate while honoring its heritage.

Iconic Collections

Calatrava

Introduced in 1932, the Calatrava represents the Platonic ideal of the dress watch. Its clean, round case and elegant simplicity have made it the template for countless imitators. The name comes from the Calatrava cross—the brand's logo—which itself derives from a 12th-century Spanish military order.

Nautilus

When Gérald Genta designed the Nautilus in 1976, he created a watch that shouldn't have worked—a luxury sports watch with an integrated bracelet and porthole-inspired case. Yet it became an icon. Today, the Nautilus is one of the most sought-after watches in the world, with waiting lists stretching years and secondary market prices reaching multiples of retail.

Aquanaut

Launched in 1997 as a more contemporary, accessible alternative to the Nautilus, the Aquanaut features a rounded octagonal case and tropical rubber strap. It has since developed its own devoted following and commands similar demand.

Grand Complications

Patek Philippe's Grand Complications collection showcases the brand's technical mastery. These watches feature perpetual calendars, minute repeaters, tourbillons, and combinations thereof. The complications are executed with a level of finishing that sets the standard for the industry.

The Patek Philippe Seal

In 2009, Patek Philippe introduced its own quality seal, replacing the Geneva Seal. The Patek Philippe Seal imposes stricter standards than any external certification, covering not just the movement but the entire watch, including its long-term performance. Only Patek Philippe watches can bear this seal—a testament to the brand's confidence in its own standards.

Record-Breaking Auctions

Patek Philippe dominates the auction world like no other brand. The most expensive watches ever sold at auction are overwhelmingly Patek Philippes:

  • $31.19 million — Grandmaster Chime (2019), the most expensive watch ever sold
  • $24.4 million — Henry Graves Supercomplication (2014)
  • $17.8 million — Steel Ref. 1518 (2016), the most expensive wristwatch at the time
  • $11 million — Ref. 2499 First Series (2018)

These prices reflect not just rarity but the profound respect collectors have for Patek Philippe's craftsmanship and heritage. A Patek Philippe isn't just a watch—it's a store of value, often appreciating over time.

Why Patek Philippe Commands Such Prestige

Uncompromising Craftsmanship

Every Patek Philippe watch requires between 9 months and over 2 years to complete. Master craftsmen hand-finish every component to standards visible only under magnification. The brand employs rare decorative techniques—guilloché, enameling, engraving—that few workshops in the world can execute at this level.

In-House Excellence

Unlike many "luxury" brands that purchase movements from third parties, Patek Philippe manufactures virtually everything in-house. This vertical integration allows for total quality control and enables innovations impossible for less integrated manufacturers.

Deliberate Scarcity

Patek Philippe produces approximately 62,000 watches per year—a fraction of what larger brands manufacture. This limited production, combined with overwhelming demand, creates scarcity that drives both desirability and value retention.

Lifetime Service Commitment

Patek Philippe promises to service any watch it has ever made, regardless of age. The brand maintains archives of every piece produced and stocks parts for watches over a century old. This commitment to perpetual support reinforces the "generation to generation" philosophy.

The Investment Perspective

While watches should never be purchased purely as investments, Patek Philippe has demonstrated remarkable value retention and appreciation. Certain references—particularly complicated pieces, limited editions, and discontinued models—have appreciated significantly over time.

Factors that influence investment potential include:

  • Reference rarity — Limited production runs command premiums
  • Condition — Original, unpolished cases with complete documentation
  • Provenance — Watches with notable previous owners
  • Complications — More complex watches tend to appreciate more
  • Material — Steel Pateks often command higher premiums than gold

The Future of Patek Philippe

As Patek Philippe approaches its 200th anniversary in 2039, the brand faces the challenge of maintaining its standards while meeting unprecedented demand. Under Thierry Stern's leadership, Patek Philippe has cautiously increased production while refusing to compromise on quality.

The brand continues to innovate technically—recent developments include silicon components for improved accuracy and new calibers with extended power reserves. Yet it remains committed to traditional craftsmanship, employing artisans in disciplines that have otherwise nearly disappeared.

In an era of smart watches and disposable technology, Patek Philippe represents something increasingly rare: permanence. A Patek Philippe made today will still be running—and likely still in demand—a century from now. That's not just watchmaking. That's creating objects for eternity.

"Begin your own tradition."

For those fortunate enough to acquire a Patek Philippe, the brand's invitation is simple: you're not just buying a watch. You're beginning a tradition that will outlast you, connecting past and future through an object of enduring beauty and precision.
