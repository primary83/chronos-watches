The History of Junghans

January 2025 · 10 min read

Junghans was once the world's largest clock and watch manufacturer, producing millions of timepieces annually. Today, the brand is celebrated for its Bauhaus-inspired Max Bill collection—perhaps the purest expression of German design philosophy on a wrist.

Black Forest Giant

Erhard Junghans founded his company in 1861 in Schramberg, in Germany's Black Forest region. Starting with clock components, Junghans grew rapidly by embracing American mass production techniques—unusual for German manufacturers who favored traditional craft methods.

By the early 20th century, Junghans had become the world's largest clock manufacturer, employing over 3,000 workers and producing millions of timepieces annually. Their factory in Schramberg was a small city unto itself.

Precision and Innovation

Junghans wasn't just about volume—they pursued precision. Their chronometers earned top marks in observatory trials, and they developed sophisticated movement technologies. The company produced chronographs for the German military and timing equipment for the 1936 and 1972 Olympic Games.

This combination of scale and quality made Junghans a powerhouse of German industry.

The Max Bill Collaboration

In 1956, Junghans began a collaboration that would define their modern identity. Max Bill, a Swiss architect, designer, and former Bauhaus student, designed a series of kitchen clocks and wall clocks for Junghans. In 1961, he created a wristwatch using the same design principles.

The Max Bill watch embodied Bauhaus ideals: pure functionality expressed through clean geometry, legible typography, and the absence of unnecessary decoration. Every element served a purpose; nothing was arbitrary.

The Max Bill Design

The Max Bill watch featured a slightly domed crystal, a slim case, numerals designed specifically for the dial, and hands that Bill himself specified in exact proportions. The result was a watch of extraordinary visual harmony—each element in perfect balance with the others.

Unlike retro designs that merely evoke a period aesthetic, the Max Bill represents authentic mid-century modernism, designed by one of the movement's masters and still produced essentially unchanged.

Quartz and Crisis

The quartz revolution hit Junghans hard. The company that had built its reputation on mechanical precision struggled to compete with cheap Asian quartz watches. Through the 1970s and 1980s, Junghans underwent multiple ownership changes and restructurings.

However, Junghans also innovated in quartz technology, developing radio-controlled watches that automatically synchronized with atomic clock signals—ensuring perfect accuracy without any user adjustment.

Revival and Focus

In 2009, the company was acquired by Hans-Jochem and Hannes Steim, members of a local Schramberg business family. Under their ownership, Junghans refocused on its strengths: the Max Bill collection, German manufacturing, and a combination of mechanical and innovative quartz technologies.

Meister Collection

The Meister collection represents Junghans' more traditional German watchmaking, with automatic movements, refined finishing, and classic proportions. These watches appeal to buyers who appreciate German engineering without the stark minimalism of Max Bill designs.

Form Collection

The Form collection brings Max Bill's design philosophy to more accessible price points, offering Bauhaus aesthetics with quartz movements. These watches make the design legacy available to buyers who prioritize the visual experience over mechanical complexity.

Junghans Today

Junghans continues to manufacture in Schramberg, maintaining connections to both their industrial heritage and design legacy. For buyers seeking authentic Bauhaus design—not an interpretation or homage, but the genuine article—Junghans offers something no other brand can: Max Bill's original vision, still in production, still made in Germany.
