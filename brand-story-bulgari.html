The History of Bulgari

January 2025 · 13 min read

"Italian Magnificence." While Swiss brands dominated watchmaking for centuries, Bulgari brought something different: Roman boldness, Italian design, and a jeweler's eye for proportion. Today, the Octo Finissimo has shattered world records for thinness, proving that this Roman house can compete at the highest levels of haute horlogerie.

The Greek Silversmith

The Bulgari story begins not in Rome, but in the village of Paramythia in northern Greece. Sotirios Voulgaris (later Italianized to Bulgari) was born in 1857 into a family of silversmiths. Political instability eventually drove him to Italy, where he arrived in the 1880s with little more than his craft skills and ambition.

After working in Naples, Sotirios moved to Rome in 1884, opening a small shop selling silver objets d'art. His Greek aesthetic—bold, sculptural, unafraid of color—stood out against the more restrained French jewelry that dominated the era. Roman aristocracy took notice, and Bulgari's reputation grew.

The Via Condotti Landmark

In 1905, Sotirios opened the flagship store at Via Condotti 10, facing the Spanish Steps. This location would become one of luxury retail's most famous addresses. The ornate interior, designed to showcase Bulgari's increasingly elaborate jewelry, attracted the international elite visiting Rome.

After Sotirios's death in 1932, his sons Constantino and Giorgio expanded the business. They developed what would become Bulgari's signature style: bold designs inspired by Greek and Roman art, generous use of colored gemstones, and an unmistakable sense of Italian glamour.

Hollywood on the Tiber

In the 1950s and 1960s, Rome's Cinecittà studios attracted Hollywood productions, and their stars discovered Bulgari. Elizabeth Taylor, famously given spectacular jewels by Richard Burton, became an unofficial ambassador. Sophia Loren, Audrey Hepburn, and other icons of the era adorned themselves with Bulgari creations.

"The only word Elizabeth knows in Italian is Bulgari."
— Richard Burton

This celebrity association established Bulgari as a global luxury name. Unlike the discreet Parisian jewelers, Bulgari embraced glamour and visibility. The brand became synonymous with la dolce vita—the sweet life of Roman elegance.

Enter Watchmaking

Bulgari began producing watches in the 1940s, initially using Swiss movements in jeweled cases. The watches were beautiful but unremarkable technically—fashion accessories rather than serious timepieces. This would change dramatically in the following decades.

In 1977, Bulgari introduced the Bulgari Bulgari—a watch featuring the brand name engraved twice on the bezel. This bold design statement broke jewelry convention, transforming the logo from discreet signature into design element. The Bulgari Bulgari became an icon of 1980s luxury.

Building Technical Credibility

The early 2000s marked Bulgari's transformation from jeweler-who-makes-watches to serious watchmaker. The acquisitions of Daniel Roth and Gérald Genta in 2000 brought genuine horological expertise. Gérald Genta himself was legendary—the designer of the Royal Oak and Nautilus. His manufacture provided technical capabilities that Bulgari lacked.

Bulgari consolidated these acquisitions into a single manufacture in Saignelégier, Switzerland. For the first time, the brand could develop and produce movements in-house. The foundation for serious watchmaking was laid.

The Gérald Genta Acquisition

  • Technical Expertise: Decades of complicated movement development
  • Manufacturing: Capability to produce in-house calibers
  • Design Heritage: Legacy of the Royal Oak designer
  • Complications: Tourbillons, minute repeaters, grande sonneries

Octo: The New Icon

In 2012, Bulgari introduced the Octo—a watch inspired by Rome's maxentius basilica and the geometric patterns of ancient architecture. The case featured 110 facets, creating a distinctive octagonal shape that managed to be both angular and comfortable on the wrist. Design director Fabrizio Buonamassa had created something genuinely new.

The Octo was impressive, but its "Finissimo" variants would shock the watch world. Beginning in 2014, Bulgari embarked on a campaign of ultra-thinness that would accumulate world records at an unprecedented pace.

The Finissimo Records

Between 2014 and 2022, Bulgari's Octo Finissimo set eight consecutive world records for thin watches:

Octo Finissimo World Records

  • 2014: Thinnest tourbillon (5.00mm)
  • 2016: Thinnest minute repeater (6.85mm)
  • 2017: Thinnest automatic movement (2.23mm) / watch (5.15mm)
  • 2018: Thinnest automatic tourbillon (3.95mm)
  • 2019: Thinnest chronograph GMT (6.90mm)
  • 2020: Thinnest automatic with tourbillon and chronograph
  • 2021: Thinnest perpetual calendar (5.80mm)
  • 2022: Thinnest mechanical watch ever—Ultra at 1.80mm

The Octo Finissimo Ultra, at just 1.80mm thick, pushed mechanical watchmaking to its absolute limits. The movement is integrated into the caseback; the barrel is spread across the entire diameter; the crown is embedded in the case edge. It's as much engineering statement as functional timepiece.

Key Collections

Octo

The architectural icon inspired by Roman geometry. Available from the sporty Octo Roma to the record-breaking Finissimo, this collection represents Bulgari's serious watchmaking credentials. The 110-facet case has become instantly recognizable.

Serpenti

The snake motif dates to 1940s Bulgari jewelry. Serpenti watches wrap the wrist in coiled bracelets, combining jewelry craft with timekeeping. These feminine pieces range from diamond-set haute joaillerie to more accessible steel versions.

Bulgari Bulgari

The 1977 original, updated for modern tastes. The double-logo bezel remains distinctive, now available in various sizes and materials. This collection balances heritage recognition with contemporary refinement.

Lvcea

Meaning "light" in Latin, Lvcea targets women who want serious watches with feminine proportions. The collection includes complications unusual in women's watchmaking—tourbillons, skeleton movements—without sacrificing elegance.

The LVMH Era

In 2011, LVMH acquired Bulgari, adding the Italian house to a portfolio that includes TAG Heuer, Hublot, and Zenith. The acquisition provided resources for continued investment in watchmaking capabilities while maintaining Bulgari's Roman identity.

Under LVMH, Bulgari has accelerated its horological ambitions. The record-breaking Finissimo campaign, expanded manufacture capabilities, and increased emphasis on in-house movements all reflect the parent company's commitment to establishing Bulgari among serious watchmakers.

What Defines Bulgari

  • Roman Heritage: Founded 1884 on Via Condotti
  • Italian Design: Bold aesthetics distinct from Swiss conservatism
  • Octo Architecture: 110-facet case inspired by Rome's geometry
  • Ultra-Thin Mastery: Eight consecutive world records
  • Jeweler's Eye: Attention to proportion, color, and finish
  • Manufacturing: In-house capabilities acquired and expanded

The Italian Difference

Bulgari brings something unique to haute horlogerie: Italian perspective. While Swiss brands emphasize precision and tradition, Bulgari emphasizes design and boldness. The Octo doesn't look like anything from Geneva or Le Locle—it looks like Rome, ancient and modern simultaneously.

This design-forward approach resonates with collectors who find Swiss watchmaking too conservative. The Finissimo records prove technical capability, while the distinctive aesthetics provide emotional appeal. It's a combination few brands achieve.

The Legacy Continues

From a Greek silversmith's Roman workshop to record-breaking haute horlogerie, Bulgari's journey spans over 140 years of evolution. The brand that dressed Elizabeth Taylor now competes directly with Switzerland's finest watchmakers—and wins.

For collectors who appreciate design as much as mechanics, who value Italian brio alongside Swiss precision, Bulgari offers something genuinely different. The Octo Finissimo has earned its place among the great watches of our era, while Serpenti and Bulgari Bulgari continue appealing to those who want glamour with their timekeeping. In a world of Swiss homogeneity, Bulgari's Roman voice rings distinctive and clear.
