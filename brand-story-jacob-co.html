The History of Jacob & Co

January 2, 2025 · 10 min read

From a small booth in Manhattan's Diamond District to creating million-dollar astronomical complications, Jacob & Co has written one of the most unlikely success stories in watchmaking. This is how "Jacob the Jeweler" became a horological force.

Jacob & Co defies easy categorization. The brand emerged from hip-hop culture and celebrity jewelry, yet now produces some of the most technically ambitious timepieces in the world. It's a journey from blinged-out excess to genuine haute horlogerie—without ever abandoning the showmanship that made it famous.

Jacob the Jeweler

Jacob Arabo was born in Uzbekistan in 1965 and immigrated to New York as a teenager. He learned jewelry making and in 1986 opened a small booth in Manhattan's Diamond District—the legendary stretch of 47th Street where the world's diamond trade converges.

Arabo's talent was evident, and his custom pieces attracted attention. But his big break came from an unexpected source: the emerging hip-hop industry. Artists seeking distinctive, statement-making jewelry discovered Jacob's ability to create pieces as bold as their personas.

The Hip-Hop Connection

By the mid-1990s, Jacob Arabo had become "Jacob the Jeweler"—the go-to source for rap royalty's most extravagant pieces. Jay-Z, Notorious B.I.G., Puff Daddy, and countless others wore Jacob's creations. His name appeared in lyrics, cementing his cultural status.

The signature piece was the "Five Time Zone" watch—a single watch displaying five different time zones simultaneously, often encrusted with diamonds. It was impractical, excessive, and completely irresistible to a clientele that valued statement over subtlety.

"I came to America with nothing. Everything I have, I built with my hands. That's the American dream." — Jacob Arabo

Evolution into Haute Horlogerie

Beyond Bling

Around 2010, Jacob & Co began a remarkable transformation. Rather than remaining solely a purveyor of diamond-encrusted fashion watches, the brand started developing genuinely complex mechanical timepieces.

Working with Swiss complications specialists, Jacob & Co created astronomical watches, tourbillons, and technical marvels that earned respect from serious collectors. The brand that started with bedazzled quartz movements was now producing calibers of genuine horological significance.

The Astronomia

The Astronomia collection represents Jacob & Co's horological ambitions at their most extreme. These watches feature a four-arm structure rotating in the center of a sapphire sphere, with a three-axis tourbillon, rotating globe, rotating moon phase, and a Jacob-cut diamond—all in constant motion.

Prices reach seven figures. Production is measured in single digits annually. And the technical achievement is genuine—these are among the most complex watches produced anywhere.

The Astronomia Solar

Perhaps the ultimate Astronomia features planets of the solar system orbiting a central sun. Mars is represented by actual meteorite from Mars. The entire planetary system rotates, with different planets taking different durations to complete their orbits. It's a wearable orrery—an astronomical instrument rendered in gold and gems.

Modern Collections

Astronomia: The flagship haute horlogerie line featuring gravitational tourbillons, rotating complications, and astronomical displays. Prices typically exceed $500,000.

Epic X: More accessible (relatively speaking) skeletonized watches showcasing mechanical movements. Entry point to Jacob & Co mechanical watchmaking.

Billionaire: Diamond-set pieces continuing the brand's jewelry heritage. The Billionaire Watch featured 260 carats of emerald-cut diamonds in an $18 million creation.

Twin Turbo Furious: Watches featuring "twin" triple-axis tourbillons—two independent tourbillon systems in a single watch. Among the most complex movements in production.

Opera: Musical watches featuring minute repeaters and automata—mechanical figures that move when the striking mechanism activates.

Five Time Zone: The original signature design, still produced with various complications and decoration levels.

The Bugatti Collaboration

Jacob & Co's partnership with Bugatti exemplifies the brand's approach: take something already extreme and push it further. The Twin Turbo Furious Bugatti features a miniature representation of Bugatti's W16 engine, complete with moving pistons visible through the dial.

The mechanical complexity required to animate the engine replica while maintaining timekeeping function demonstrates genuine horological capability. It's showmanship, yes—but showmanship backed by serious engineering.

Critics and Fans

Jacob & Co remains polarizing. Traditional watch collectors often dismiss the brand as excessive, more focused on visual impact than horological purity. The hip-hop origins and celebrity associations seem incompatible with serious watchmaking to some.

Yet the Astronomia's technical achievements are undeniable. The brand works with respected Swiss complications specialists. The movements are genuinely complex, not just decorated. And the willingness to create pieces that existing brands wouldn't dare consider has produced genuinely unique timepieces.

Perhaps Jacob & Co's contribution is proving that watchmaking can accommodate more than one aesthetic. Not every great watch needs to be understated. There's room for spectacle alongside sobriety.

The Legacy

Jacob Arabo's journey from Diamond District booth to haute horlogerie manufacture is uniquely American. The brand emerged from a specific cultural moment—hip-hop's rise to mainstream dominance—and evolved as its founder's ambitions expanded.

Today, Jacob & Co bridges worlds that rarely overlap. The same company produces diamond-encrusted fashion pieces and million-dollar astronomical complications. It counts rappers and serious collectors among its clientele. It's dismissed as vulgar and praised as innovative, often by the same people looking at different pieces.

That's Jacob & Co's achievement: refusing to be one thing. The brand that made its name on excess has proven it can also do substance—without ever apologizing for the excess that built it.
