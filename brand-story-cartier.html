The History of Cartier

January 2, 2025 · 12 min read

"The jeweler of kings and the king of jewelers." Edward VII's famous description of Cartier captures the essence of a house that has adorned royalty for over 175 years—and invented the modern wristwatch along the way.

Cartier occupies a unique position in the watch world. Unlike brands that evolved from Swiss workshops, Cartier began as a Parisian jeweler and brought a jeweler's eye to watchmaking. The result: timepieces that prioritize aesthetic perfection over mechanical complexity, and designs so influential they've been copied by virtually every brand in existence.

The Founding

In 1847, Louis-François Cartier took over the jewelry workshop of his master, Adolphe Picard, at 29 Rue Montorgueil in Paris. It was a modest beginning in a city already filled with jewelers, but Louis-François had ambition and, crucially, taste that appealed to the emerging bourgeoisie of Napoleon III's Second Empire.

His son Alfred joined the business in 1874 and later brought in his own sons: Louis, Pierre, and Jacques. These three brothers would transform Cartier from a successful Parisian jeweler into a global luxury empire with boutiques in Paris, London, and New York.

The Royal Connection

Cartier's association with royalty began in 1856 when Princess Mathilde, cousin of Emperor Napoleon III, made her first purchase. The introduction to the imperial court opened doors across Europe's royal houses.

In 1902, King Edward VII of England issued Cartier a royal warrant and famously declared them "the jeweler of kings and the king of jewelers." This wasn't mere flattery—Edward ordered 27 tiaras for his coronation, and Cartier supplied them all.

At various times, Cartier has held royal warrants from the courts of Spain, Portugal, Russia, Siam, Greece, Serbia, Belgium, Romania, Egypt, Albania, Monaco, and the House of Orléans. No other jeweler can claim such comprehensive royal patronage.

"I must be able to satisfy the Indian Maharajas and the American heiresses, the oilmen of Texas and the Persian princes." — Louis Cartier

Inventing the Wristwatch

The Santos: 1904

The most important moment in Cartier's watchmaking history came from a friendship. Brazilian aviator Alberto Santos-Dumont was a celebrity in early 1900s Paris, famous for his daring flights in dirigibles and early airplanes. He was also a friend of Louis Cartier.

Santos-Dumont complained that checking his pocket watch while piloting was nearly impossible—he needed both hands to control the aircraft. Louis Cartier's solution was revolutionary: a flat watch with a leather strap designed to be worn on the wrist.

The Santos, created in 1904, wasn't technically the first wristwatch, but it was among the first designed specifically for men and the first created as a practical tool rather than decorative jewelry. Its square case with exposed screws was radically modern—a design so successful it remains in Cartier's collection today, largely unchanged.

The Santos

Introduced 1904

The first pilot's watch and one of the first wristwatches designed for men. Its square case and exposed screws broke from round pocket watch tradition, creating a design language still influential 120 years later.

The Tank: 1917

During World War I, Louis Cartier was struck by the Renault tanks he saw from above—their rectangular treads forming a distinctive geometric shape. He sketched a watch inspired by their form, and the Tank was born.

The design was revolutionary: the brancards (vertical side bars) weren't just decorative—they integrated the strap attachment into the case itself, creating a seamless visual flow. The Tank's rectangular shape was perfectly proportioned according to the golden ratio, giving it an aesthetic harmony that transcends fashion.

Cartier presented one of the first Tank watches to General John Pershing, commander of American forces in Europe. The watch has since been worn by an extraordinary roster of cultural icons: Andy Warhol, Jackie Kennedy, Princess Diana, Muhammad Ali, and Yves Saint Laurent, among countless others.

The Tank

Introduced 1917

Inspired by WWI Renault tanks, the Tank became the ultimate symbol of refined elegance. Its perfect proportions and integrated design have made it the most copied watch shape in history.

The Art Deco Years

The 1920s and 30s were Cartier's golden age of creativity. The house became synonymous with Art Deco, producing jewelry and watches that defined the era's geometric elegance.

Louis Cartier developed the "mystery clock"—timepieces with hands that appeared to float in transparent dials with no visible mechanism. He created watches in every conceivable shape: tortoises, chimeras, and Egyptian motifs inspired by the discovery of Tutankhamun's tomb.

Iconic Art Deco Creations

The Baignoire (1912): An elongated oval case that resembles a bathtub (baignoire in French), demonstrating Cartier's willingness to explore unconventional forms.

The Tonneau (1906): A barrel-shaped case that predated the Tank and established Cartier's taste for geometric cases.

The Crash (1967): A "melted" watch case supposedly inspired by a warped Cartier watch retrieved from a car accident. Salvador Dalí reportedly loved it.

The Modern Era

The Panthère: 1983

Cartier's panther motif dates to 1914, but the Panthère watch didn't arrive until 1983. Its fully integrated bracelet with Art Deco-influenced links became the definitive luxury sports watch of the 1980s and remains popular today after its 2017 relaunch.

The Ballon Bleu: 2007

Cartier's first new design icon in decades, the Ballon Bleu features a round case with a distinctive cabochon crown guard. Its convex curves gave Cartier a modern classic that appeals to contemporary tastes while maintaining the house's design DNA.

Manufacture Watchmaking

For most of its history, Cartier was a "habilleur"—a company that designed cases and dials but sourced movements from Swiss suppliers. This changed in 2002 when Cartier established its own movement manufacture.

Today, Cartier produces in-house calibers for its haute horlogerie pieces, including skeleton movements, tourbillons, and the remarkable Mysterious Hour movements that continue Louis Cartier's legacy of mystery clocks.

The Cartier Difference

What sets Cartier apart from Swiss watch brands? Fundamentally, it's a matter of philosophy. Swiss watchmakers typically begin with movement engineering and build outward; Cartier begins with design and ensures the movement serves the aesthetic vision.

This approach produces watches that prioritize visual harmony over mechanical bragging rights. A Cartier Tank doesn't compete with a Rolex on water resistance or a Patek on finishing—it competes on pure design elegance, and on that measure, it has few rivals.

The Legacy

Today, Cartier operates as part of the Richemont group, alongside IWC, Jaeger-LeCoultre, and other prestigious brands. But it maintains a distinct identity: the jeweler's eye, the Parisian elegance, and the willingness to put beauty before mechanism.

The Santos and Tank remain in production, still recognizable from their original designs. They're worn by celebrities and civilians alike, still conveying the same message they did a century ago: that true elegance transcends time.

That's the real Cartier legacy—not just inventing the wristwatch, but proving that watches could be art. Every brand that's ever considered how a watch looks on the wrist owes something to Louis Cartier and his vision of the timepiece as jewelry.
