Buying Guide

Best GMT Watches Compared

January 2025 · 14 min read

GMT watches display two time zones simultaneously—essential for travelers, professionals with international contacts, or anyone who needs to track time elsewhere. Here are the best GMT watches at every price point.

What Makes a Great GMT?

A GMT watch features a fourth hand (the GMT hand) that completes one rotation every 24 hours, combined with a 24-hour bezel or scale. This allows tracking a second time zone. True "caller" GMTs allow independent adjustment of the local hour hand, while "office" GMTs adjust the GMT hand instead.

Budget: Under $500

Orient Star GMT — Around $450. Orient delivers an in-house automatic GMT at a remarkable price. The power reserve indicator adds practicality. For value-focused buyers, Orient proves GMT complications don't require luxury pricing.

Seiko 5 Sports GMT (SSK series) — Around $400-475. Seiko's accessible GMT features their 4R34 movement with jumping hour hand. Multiple colorways reference classic GMT bezels. The value proposition is exceptional.

Glycine Airman — Around $500-700. The original pilot's GMT watch with genuine aviation heritage. Glycine pioneered the 24-hour dial format. Now under Invicta ownership, prices have become very accessible.

Budget: $500-2,000

Tissot PRX Powermatic 80 GMT — Around $750. Tissot brings GMT functionality to their popular PRX case with Swiss automatic movement and 80-hour power reserve. Modern styling with practical travel utility.

Mido Ocean Star GMT — Around $1,100. Swiss automatic GMT with ceramic bezel and 80-hour power reserve. The Ocean Star offers genuine tool watch capability at Swatch Group value pricing.

Longines Spirit Zulu Time — Around $2,500-2,900. Longines delivers serious GMT capability with flyer-style aesthetics and COSC certification. The silicon balance spring ensures reliability. Exceptional value for Swiss luxury GMT.

Budget: $2,000-5,000

Tudor Black Bay GMT — Around $4,100. Tudor's GMT offers Rolex DNA with in-house movement at accessible luxury pricing. The distinctive "Pepsi" bezel references GMT heritage while the snowflake hands provide Tudor identity. 70-hour power reserve adds practicality.

Oris Aquis GMT — Around $2,500-3,000. Oris delivers independent Swiss watchmaking with genuine dive capability (300m) plus GMT function. The rotating inner bezel protects against accidental adjustment.

Grand Seiko SBGN003 — Around $3,000. Grand Seiko's quartz GMT offers extraordinary accuracy (±10 seconds per year) with the brand's legendary finishing. For travelers prioritizing precision over mechanical romance, this is compelling.

Budget: $5,000-10,000

Omega Seamaster Aqua Terra GMT — Around $6,500-7,500. Omega's travel watch combines Master Chronometer certification, 15,000 gauss antimagnetic resistance, and versatile styling. The Aqua Terra works from boardroom to beach.

Grand Seiko SBGE257 (Spring Drive GMT) — Around $6,500. Spring Drive's unique technology delivers ±1 second per day accuracy with that mesmerizing glide-motion seconds hand. The GMT function adds travel utility to Grand Seiko's exceptional finishing.

Breitling Navitimer Automatic GMT — Around $6,000-7,000. The pilot's watch icon with GMT complication. The slide rule bezel and aviation heritage appeal to those who appreciate functional complexity.

Budget: $10,000+

Rolex GMT-Master II — Around $10,500+ retail (significantly higher on secondary market). The original luxury GMT, developed for Pan Am pilots in 1955. The "Pepsi," "Batman," and "Coke" bezels are cultural icons. Availability remains the main challenge—finding one at retail requires patience or purchase history.

Omega Speedmaster Moonwatch GMT — Around $11,000. The Moonwatch with GMT complication adds travel functionality to space heritage. Co-Axial movement provides Omega's latest technology.

Jaeger-LeCoultre Polaris GMT — Around $10,500. JLC's sports GMT offers manufacture movement, exceptional finishing, and the Polaris collection's distinctive alarm-watch-inspired design.

True GMT vs Office GMT

True "Caller" GMT: The local hour hand jumps independently, allowing quick adjustment when crossing time zones while the GMT hand maintains home time. Found in Rolex GMT-Master II, Tudor Black Bay GMT, and higher-end models.

"Office" GMT: The GMT hand adjusts independently while the local time adjusts traditionally. Better for tracking a fixed remote location (like your home office) rather than frequent travel.

Choosing Your GMT

Consider how you'll use it. Frequent travelers benefit from true caller GMTs. Professionals tracking a fixed second location work fine with office GMTs. If you rarely cross time zones but like the aesthetic, any GMT works—the complication adds visual interest regardless of travel frequency.

Best Value GMT

The Tudor Black Bay GMT offers the best combination of heritage, in-house movement, and practical functionality under $5,000. For those seeking exceptional value below $500, the Seiko 5 Sports GMT punches well above its weight.

Best Overall GMT

The Rolex GMT-Master II remains the benchmark—if you can find one. For those wanting attainable excellence, the Omega Seamaster Aqua Terra GMT delivers Master Chronometer quality at reasonable pricing with actual availability.
