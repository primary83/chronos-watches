Key Features to Consider

Water Resistance

100m is fine for swimming and snorkeling. 200m handles recreational scuba. 300m+ is overkill for most people but provides extra peace of mind. Beyond that, you're paying for engineering bragging rights.

Bezel Type

Unidirectional bezels are standard for dive watchesâ€”they can only move counterclockwise, so an accidental bump won't show more remaining dive time than you actually have. Ceramic bezels resist scratches; aluminum bezels develop attractive patina.

Size Matters

Classic dive watches were 38-40mm. Modern ones trend larger (42-44mm). Consider your wrist sizeâ€”a massive diver can look cartoonish on a slim wrist. When in doubt, go smaller.

Bracelet vs. Strap

Steel bracelets are versatile and durable. Rubber straps are more comfortable in water and during hot weather. Many enthusiasts own both and swap based on activity and mood.
