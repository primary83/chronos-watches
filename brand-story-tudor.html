The History of Tudor

January 2, 2025 · 11 min read

For decades, Tudor lived in Rolex's shadow—a capable but unglamorous sibling selling watches to those who couldn't afford the crown. Then something remarkable happened: Tudor stepped out of that shadow and became a collector favorite in its own right.

Tudor's story is one of reinvention. Created by Rolex founder Hans Wilsdorf to offer affordable quality, Tudor spent most of its existence as "Rolex's little brother." But a strategic repositioning in the 2010s, combined with stunning new designs and in-house movements, transformed Tudor into one of the most exciting brands in contemporary watchmaking.

The Founding Vision

Hans Wilsdorf registered the Tudor name in 1926, but the brand didn't launch until 1946. His vision was clear: create watches with Rolex-quality cases and craftsmanship but with more affordable movements sourced from other Swiss manufacturers.

"For some years now I have been considering the idea of making a watch that our agents could sell at a more modest price than our Rolex watches, and yet one that could attain the standard of dependability for which Rolex is famous," Wilsdorf wrote at the time.

The strategy worked brilliantly. Tudor watches used Rolex cases, crowns, and crystals, but powered by reliable (and less expensive) movements from suppliers like ETA. The result: genuine Rolex build quality at roughly half the price.

"Tudor watches are made to the same specifications as Rolex watches, with the same meticulous care and identical materials, but with a different movement." — Early Tudor advertising

Tool Watch Heritage

The Military Connection

Tudor's reputation was built on professional use. The French Marine Nationale adopted Tudor dive watches in the 1950s and used them for decades. The U.S. Navy tested Tudor Submariners alongside Rolex models. These weren't marketing partnerships—they were genuine military procurement decisions based on capability and value.

The Submariner Era

Tudor introduced its own Submariner in 1954, featuring the distinctive "rose" logo that characterized early Tudor watches. These watches shared cases with Rolex Submariners but cost significantly less—and they proved just as reliable in professional diving applications.

Vintage Tudor Submariners and "Snowflake" models (named for their distinctive hands) have become highly collectible, often commanding prices that exceed what they originally cost relative to Rolex.

The Modern Renaissance

Heritage Black Bay: 2012

Everything changed with the Heritage Black Bay. Tudor reached into its archives and created a modern dive watch that honored the brand's history while feeling entirely contemporary. The "big crown" from Tudor's 1958 reference 7924, the "snowflake" hands, the gilt dial details—every element referenced Tudor heritage.

Crucially, it wasn't a Rolex knockoff. The Black Bay had its own identity, its own aesthetic, its own appeal. Collectors responded enthusiastically, and suddenly Tudor wasn't just "affordable Rolex"—it was a desirable brand in its own right.

In-House Movements: 2015

Tudor's declaration of independence came in 2015 with the MT5612 movement. For the first time, Tudor watches ran on calibers developed and produced specifically for Tudor—not borrowed from ETA or anyone else.

The MT5612 offered 70-hour power reserve (versus ETA's 40 hours), COSC certification, and a silicon balance spring. Tudor had moved from value proposition to technical innovation.

Black Bay 58: 2018

The Black Bay 58 perfected the formula. At 39mm with vintage-inspired proportions, it addressed the main criticism of the original Black Bay (too thick at 14.8mm). The BB58's slimmer 11.9mm case and compact 47.5mm lug-to-lug made it the dive watch for people who found modern divers too chunky.

It became arguably the most recommended watch in the enthusiast community—the answer to "what's a great watch under $4,000?"

The Pelagos: Technical Excellence

While the Black Bay mined heritage, the Pelagos showed Tudor could innovate. Launched in 2012, this titanium dive watch was designed as a legitimate professional tool:

• 500m water resistance
• Titanium case and bracelet
• Helium escape valve
• Self-adjusting clasp that compensates for wetsuit compression
• Fully luminous dial

The Pelagos proved Tudor wasn't just a heritage brand—it could compete on pure technical merit with any dive watch in existence.

Brand Identity Today

Tudor's current positioning is sophisticated: it's not "affordable Rolex" anymore. It's a brand with its own identity, its own design language, and its own devoted following.

"Born to Dare" became Tudor's tagline, emphasizing adventure and independence rather than value. Marketing features ambassadors like David Beckham and Lady Gaga—not the establishment figures associated with Rolex, but creative risk-takers.

The watches reflect this identity shift. Tudor models have become bolder, more colorful, more willing to experiment. The Black Bay Chrono, the Black Bay GMT, the Ranger—each offers something distinctly Tudor rather than derivative of Rolex.

The Value Proposition

Tudor's modern positioning creates an interesting value equation. You're not buying "Rolex lite"—you're buying a watch from a brand with genuine heritage, manufacture movements, and its own aesthetic vision. The connection to Rolex provides manufacturing excellence and servicing infrastructure, but the product stands on its own merits.

For many collectors, Tudor represents the sweet spot: serious watchmaking credentials without Rolex prices or waitlists. You can walk into most authorized dealers and actually purchase the watch you want—a novelty in today's market.

The Legacy

Tudor's journey from Rolex's affordable alternative to respected independent brand is one of the great repositioning stories in luxury goods. By embracing rather than hiding from its heritage, and by investing in genuine technical development, Tudor became something its founder might not have imagined: a brand that collectors choose over Rolex, not instead of it.

Hans Wilsdorf created Tudor to bring quality watchmaking to more people. Today, that mission continues—but with a confidence and identity that makes Tudor a destination brand, not a compromise. That's perhaps the greatest tribute to Wilsdorf's original vision.
