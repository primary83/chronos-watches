What to Do If Water Gets Inside Your Watch

January 2025 · 8 min read

You see condensation under the crystal. Your heart sinks. Water has breached your watch's defenses. What you do in the next few hours can mean the difference between a minor repair and a ruined movement.

Act Immediately

Time is your enemy. Corrosion begins within hours of water intrusion and accelerates rapidly. The moment you notice moisture inside your watch, stop wearing it and begin emergency measures. Every hour counts.

Don't assume a small amount of condensation is harmless. Even minimal moisture can cause significant damage to the delicate components of a mechanical movement—hairsprings, pivots, and jewels can all be affected.

Emergency Steps

  • Step 1: Remove the watch immediately and dry the exterior
  • Step 2: If possible, unscrew the crown (don't pull it out)
  • Step 3: Place watch in dry rice or silica gel packets
  • Step 4: Contact a watchmaker—same day if possible
  • Step 5: Don't try to operate the crown or pushers

The Rice Method

While not a cure, placing your watch in a container of uncooked rice can help absorb moisture as a temporary measure. Silica gel packets (the kind that come with new shoes or electronics) work even better. Seal the watch in a container with the desiccant and leave it overnight.

Important: This is first aid, not treatment. Even if the condensation disappears, your watch needs professional attention. Moisture may remain in the movement even when not visible, and corrosion has likely already begun.

What NOT to Do

Don't use heat. Hair dryers, ovens, or radiators can damage gaskets, crack crystals, and harm the movement. Heat causes rapid expansion that can worsen damage.

Don't open the caseback yourself. Without proper tools and a clean environment, you'll likely introduce dust and debris that cause additional problems. Leave this to professionals.

Don't shake or tap the watch. This won't remove water and may spread it to previously dry areas of the movement.

Don't wait and see. "Maybe it will dry out" is how minor water damage becomes major water damage.

Professional Assessment

A watchmaker will open the case, assess the extent of moisture intrusion, and determine what needs to be done. Best case scenario: the movement can be dried, cleaned, and re-lubricated. Worst case: significant components need replacement.

For salt water exposure, the situation is more urgent. Salt is highly corrosive and can cause severe damage within hours. If your watch was submerged in ocean water, consider it an emergency.

Repair Costs

Water damage repair costs vary dramatically based on severity. A minor case of condensation caught immediately might require only a cleaning and gasket replacement—perhaps $100-$300. Severe damage requiring dial refinishing, hand replacement, and movement restoration can run $500-$2000 or more for luxury watches.

If you have watch insurance, document the damage with photos before sending for repair. Many policies cover accidental water damage.

Prevention for the Future

After your watch is repaired, take steps to prevent a recurrence. Have water resistance tested annually. Replace gaskets during regular service. Be honest about your watch's limitations—if it's rated for splashes, don't swim with it.

Consider having a dedicated water-resistant "beater" watch for activities that risk water exposure. It's better to damage a $200 dive watch than ruin a cherished heirloom.

The Silver Lining

Many watches survive water damage with proper care and prompt professional attention. Vintage watches are regularly restored after decades in damp conditions. Modern service techniques can address even significant corrosion if caught in time. Don't give up hope—but do act fast.
