The History of Longines

January 2, 2025 · 11 min read

"Elegance is an Attitude." Longines has embodied this philosophy for over 190 years, creating watches that blend refined aesthetics with genuine technical achievement. One of the oldest registered watch trademarks in the world, the winged hourglass has graced timepieces worn by aviators, athletes, and style icons alike.

Longines occupies a unique position in the watch world: a heritage brand with genuine historical achievements in aviation and sports timing, now positioned as accessible luxury. For those who value elegant design and rich history without five-figure price tags, Longines offers exceptional value.

The Founding

In 1832, Auguste Agassiz established a watch trading business in Saint-Imier, Switzerland. The company operated as a comptoir—an établissage system where movements and components were made by independent craftsmen throughout the region and assembled under one brand name.

The transformation came in 1867 when Agassiz's nephew, Ernest Francillon, built the first Longines factory at a location called "Les Longines" (the long meadows) near Saint-Imier. This was revolutionary: instead of relying on scattered craftsmen, Francillon brought all production under one roof, creating one of the world's first modern watch manufactures.

The name "Longines" was registered as a trademark in 1889—making it one of the oldest watch trademarks still in use today. The winged hourglass logo, symbolizing time's flight, has remained virtually unchanged since then.

"We chose the name of our location: Les Longines. It was simple, easy to pronounce in any language, and it belonged to us." — Ernest Francillon

Aviation Pioneers

Timing the Skies

Longines' association with aviation began in 1919 when they became official timekeeper for the Fédération Aéronautique Internationale (FAI). This wasn't just timekeeping—it meant certifying world records for aviation achievements during the golden age of flight.

Throughout the 1920s and 30s, nearly every significant aviation record was timed by Longines instruments. The brand developed specialized pilot's watches with large, legible dials, rotating bezels for calculations, and the reliability needed when lives depended on accurate navigation.

Lindbergh and the Hour Angle Watch

In 1927, Charles Lindbergh made the first solo transatlantic flight, wearing a Longines watch. Afterward, Lindbergh collaborated with Longines to design the Hour Angle Watch—a navigation instrument that allowed pilots to determine their longitude using the watch, a sextant, and astronomical tables.

The Hour Angle Watch was worn by aviators and navigators throughout the era of pioneer aviation. Longines still produces a modern version of this iconic design, faithful to Lindbergh's original specifications.

The Weems Second-Setting Watch

Before Lindbergh, naval officer Philip Van Horn Weems collaborated with Longines on a watch featuring a rotating inner dial that allowed precise synchronization with radio time signals. This "Weems" design became standard equipment for military and commercial aviators. Both the Weems and Lindbergh designs remain in Longines' current collection.

Sports Timing Heritage

Beyond aviation, Longines built an extraordinary legacy in sports timing. The brand has served as official timekeeper for countless world championships in Alpine skiing, gymnastics, archery, and equestrian events.

Technical Innovations

Longines developed numerous timing technologies: the first electronic sports timing in 1912, the first quartz timing system in 1954, and the first fully automatic sports timing in 1966. These innovations weren't just for marketing—they advanced what precision timing could achieve.

Equestrian Connection

Longines maintains particularly strong ties to equestrian sports, serving as official partner of the FEI (Fédération Equestre Internationale) and title sponsor of major events including show jumping, eventing, and dressage. The "Longines Ranking" determines world standings in show jumping.

Modern Collections

Master Collection: Elegant dress watches featuring moon phases, retrograde displays, and annual calendars. The Master Collection represents Longines' refined, classical aesthetic at accessible prices.

Spirit: Modern pilot's watches inspired by Longines' aviation heritage. The Spirit Zulu Time offers true GMT functionality with excellent legibility—a pilot's watch that actually functions as one.

Legend Diver: A faithful reissue of Longines' 1960s dive watch, featuring the distinctive internal rotating bezel operated by a second crown. A genuine vintage reissue rather than a design exercise.

Heritage: Reissues of historical Longines models including the Sector Dial, Big Eye chronograph, and various aviation pieces. Each draws directly from specific watches in Longines' extensive archives.

Conquest: Sports watches including the VHP (Very High Precision) quartz models accurate to ±5 seconds per year—among the most accurate non-connected watches available.

DolceVita: Rectangular case watches inspired by the Art Deco era, offering an alternative to the ubiquitous round case.

The Value Proposition

Longines occupies a sweet spot in the market. Priced roughly between $1,500-$4,000 for most models, they offer:

• Swiss manufacture with genuine historical significance
• Quality movements (including some exclusive calibers)
• Elegant finishing appropriate to the price point
• Designs drawn from a legitimate archive of aviation and sports timing heritage
• The backing of the Swatch Group for service and support

For buyers who want Swiss heritage and refined aesthetics without the prices commanded by Omega (Longines' sister brand) or Rolex, Longines delivers substantial value. The Spirit Zulu Time, in particular, offers genuine GMT functionality at a price point that makes alternatives look overpriced.

The Swatch Group Era

Since joining the Swatch Group in 1983, Longines has been positioned as the group's "elegant" brand—below Omega in the hierarchy but above Tissot and Hamilton. This positioning has been remarkably consistent, allowing Longines to develop a clear identity.

Access to Swatch Group resources means Longines can develop exclusive movements (like the VHP quartz) while maintaining competitive pricing. The scale of Swatch Group production enables quality at price points smaller brands cannot match.

The Legacy

Longines' 190+ year history isn't just marketing material—it's a genuine record of horological achievement. The brand was present at the birth of aviation, timing the flights that connected continents. Their innovations in sports timing advanced the technology for everyone. Their watches accompanied explorers, athletes, and adventurers at pivotal moments.

Today, Longines offers access to that heritage without requiring a collector's budget. The watches are well-made, elegantly designed, and backed by nearly two centuries of watchmaking expertise. For anyone who values history and elegance over hype and status, Longines deserves serious consideration.

"Elegance is an Attitude" isn't just a slogan—it's a promise that refined taste doesn't require unlimited funds.
