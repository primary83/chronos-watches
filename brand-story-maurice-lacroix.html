The History of Maurice Lacroix

January 2025 · 9 min read

Maurice Lacroix proved that in-house movements and innovative design weren't exclusive to elite Swiss brands. From their base in Saignelégier, they've delivered genuine manufacture watchmaking at prices that make haute horlogerie accessible.

A Young Brand with Ambition

Maurice Lacroix was founded in 1975 in Saignelégier, in the Swiss Jura mountains. Unlike venerable houses with centuries of history, Maurice Lacroix entered the market as a newcomer with something to prove. Their mission: create sophisticated Swiss watches that competed with established names at more accessible price points.

The brand was established by Desco von Schulthess, an Austrian company with roots in watch trading dating back to 1889. They chose the name Maurice Lacroix to give the brand a distinctly Swiss identity and the gravitas of tradition.

Building Credibility

Through the 1980s and 1990s, Maurice Lacroix established itself in the "accessible luxury" segment—below the elite Swiss houses but above fashion watches. They built a reputation for reliable, well-finished watches with classical designs that appealed to professionals seeking quality without excessive pricing.

The Pontos and Les Classiques collections became pillars of the lineup, offering elegant dress watches and versatile sport models with ETA-based movements.

The Masterpiece Collection

The Masterpiece line represented Maurice Lacroix's ambitions to move upmarket. These watches featured more sophisticated complications—moonphases, retrograde displays, skeleton movements—demonstrating the brand's capacity for genuine horological complexity.

Masterpiece models showed that Maurice Lacroix could compete not just on price, but on technical merit and finishing quality.

In-House Movement Development

The major turning point came in 2006 when Maurice Lacroix developed their first in-house movement, the ML106. This made them one of the few brands in their price segment to achieve manufacture status—a significant achievement that transformed their credibility among serious collectors.

The development of proprietary movements required substantial investment in research, development, and manufacturing capability. Maurice Lacroix proved they were serious about watchmaking, not just watch marketing.

The AIKON: A Modern Icon

The AIKON collection, introduced in 2016, drew inspiration from the brand's 1990s Calypso model. With its distinctive integrated bracelet design and sporty-elegant aesthetic, the AIKON positioned Maurice Lacroix alongside the trend for luxury sports watches à la Royal Oak and Nautilus—but at a fraction of the price.

The AIKON became a commercial and critical success, demonstrating that Maurice Lacroix could create desirable, contemporary designs while maintaining their value proposition.

Innovative Complications

Maurice Lacroix has introduced several innovative complications, including the Square Wheel, which replaces the traditional round wheel with a square one, creating a visually striking indication of the watch's operation. Such innovations prove the brand's willingness to experiment and push boundaries.

Manufacturing Capability

Today, Maurice Lacroix maintains serious manufacturing capability in Saignelégier. While they still use some external movements for entry-level models, their ability to produce in-house calibers puts them in select company among brands in their price range.

The Value Equation

Maurice Lacroix occupies an interesting position in the watch market. They're too expensive to be considered entry-level, but too affordable to compete with established luxury houses. This middle ground has proven challenging for some brands, but Maurice Lacroix has succeeded by delivering genuine quality and innovation.

For collectors who want Swiss manufacture watchmaking without the premium prices of prestigious names, Maurice Lacroix offers a compelling alternative.

Maurice Lacroix Today

The brand continues to operate from Switzerland, producing watches that punch above their weight class. Whether it's the sporty AIKON, the classic Pontos, or the ambitious Masterpiece, Maurice Lacroix proves that horological excellence isn't the exclusive domain of the Swiss elite.
