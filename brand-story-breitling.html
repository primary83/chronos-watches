The History of Breitling

January 2, 2025 · 11 min read

"Instruments for Professionals." Breitling's tagline isn't marketing—it's a mission statement backed by over 140 years of building watches for pilots, divers, and adventurers who stake their lives on precision.

While other brands court celebrities and fashion, Breitling has always focused on function. Their watches were designed to be used: calculating fuel consumption at 30,000 feet, timing decompression stops at 300 meters, navigating across oceans. This commitment to professional utility has created some of the most capable—and distinctive—watches in horology.

The Founding

Léon Breitling established his workshop in Saint-Imier, Switzerland, in 1884. From the beginning, his focus was chronographs and precision instruments rather than conventional watches. By 1889, he had moved to La Chaux-de-Fonds—the heart of Swiss watchmaking—and was producing chronographs, stopwatches, and timing devices for sports, science, and industry.

Léon's son Gaston joined the company in 1914 and continued the chronograph focus, developing instruments for automobiles and aircraft. Aviation was still in its infancy, but Gaston Breitling recognized that pilots would need precise instruments to navigate the skies.

The Aviation Pioneer

Dashboard Instruments

In the 1930s and 40s, Breitling became a primary supplier of cockpit chronographs and onboard instruments for the Royal Air Force. These weren't luxury goods—they were essential flight instruments used by pilots in combat and reconnaissance missions.

This military connection established Breitling's credentials as a serious instrument maker and created the design DNA that still defines the brand: highly legible dials, rotating bezels for calculations, and robust construction that could withstand the rigors of flight.

The Navitimer: 1952

Breitling's most iconic creation is the Navitimer (navigation timer), introduced in 1952. Its distinctive feature is the circular slide rule bezel, which allows pilots to perform critical calculations: fuel consumption, rate of climb or descent, flight time, distance, and speed conversions.

The Aircraft Owners and Pilots Association (AOPA) adopted the Navitimer as their official watch, featuring their logo on the dial. For decades, the Navitimer was standard equipment for pilots worldwide—not a fashion statement, but a genuine flight instrument worn on the wrist.

"The Navitimer wasn't designed to look like a pilot's watch. It was designed to be a pilot's watch. The difference matters." — Aviation historian

How the Slide Rule Works

The Navitimer's rotating bezel contains logarithmic scales that, when aligned properly, can multiply and divide numbers. Pilots use this for calculations like: "At 180 knots, how long to cover 450 miles?" or "At 12 gallons per hour consumption, how much fuel for a 3-hour flight?" In the pre-computer era, this was essential cockpit technology.

Space and Sea

The Cosmonaute: 1962

When NASA astronaut Scott Carpenter prepared for his Mercury-Atlas 7 orbital mission, he requested a Navitimer with a 24-hour dial—in space, the normal 12-hour dial would be confusing without the visual reference of day and night. Breitling obliged, and Carpenter became the first American to wear a wristwatch in space.

The Navitimer Cosmonaute remains in production today, distinguished by its 24-hour dial that reads noon at the top and midnight at the bottom—exactly as Carpenter requested.

The Superocean: 1957

Aviation wasn't Breitling's only domain. The Superocean, launched in 1957, was designed for professional divers with 200-meter water resistance—exceptional for its era. The watch proved Breitling could build serious instruments for any environment, not just the cockpit.

Modern Superoceans reach depths of 2,000 meters, with helium escape valves and construction that meets ISO dive watch standards. They're genuine professional dive instruments, not just styled to look like them.

The Modern Era

The Chronomat Revival: 1984

By the early 1980s, the quartz crisis had weakened Breitling along with much of the Swiss industry. The brand's revival came in 1984 with the new Chronomat, developed in partnership with the Frecce Tricolori (Italian aerobatic demonstration team).

The new Chronomat was bold, masculine, and unapologetically large—a professional pilot's watch that made a statement. Its rider tabs on the bezel (small raised markers at 3, 6, 9, and 12) became a Breitling signature, and the model became the brand's best-seller.

In-House Manufacturing: 2009

For most of its history, Breitling used movements from outside suppliers. That changed in 2009 with the B01—a fully in-house chronograph movement with column-wheel mechanism and 70-hour power reserve. The B01 announced that Breitling was now a manufacture in the traditional Swiss sense, designing and producing its own calibers.

Georges Kern Era: 2017

When CVC Capital Partners acquired Breitling in 2017, they installed former IWC CEO Georges Kern as leader. Kern modernized Breitling's image while respecting its heritage: streamlined collections, updated marketing, and a new focus on sustainability (including watches made from recycled ocean plastic).

The brand's identity shifted from purely professional instruments to "casual luxury"—watches that honor their tool-watch heritage while fitting modern lifestyles. The results have been commercially successful while maintaining Breitling's technical credibility.

Iconic Collections Today

Navitimer: The legendary pilot's watch, still featuring the slide rule bezel 70 years after its debut. Available from 41mm to 46mm with various complications.

Chronomat: The bold chronograph that saved the brand in the 1980s. Modern versions offer everything from time-only to GMT and chronograph functions.

Superocean: Professional dive watches rated from 200m to 2,000m. The Superocean Heritage line adds vintage-inspired aesthetics to serious capability.

Avenger: Military-inspired pilots' watches with robust construction and extreme legibility. The most tool-focused collection in the current lineup.

Premier: Breitling's dress watch line, proving the brand can do elegance when appropriate—without abandoning its chronograph expertise.

The Legacy

Breitling's commitment to professional instruments hasn't wavered in 140 years. While the market has shifted from professional pilots and divers to enthusiasts and collectors, the watches themselves remain genuinely capable tools.

A modern Navitimer can still perform slide-rule calculations. A Superocean can still survive saturation diving. An Avenger can still endure whatever military personnel encounter. These aren't nostalgic nods to history—they're continuing proof that Breitling builds watches for people who need them to work.

That's the Breitling difference: instruments for professionals, worn by anyone who appreciates what "professional" really means.
