The History of Audemars Piguet

January 2, 2025 · 13 min read

In a world of conglomerates and corporate acquisitions, Audemars Piguet stands alone: the only major Swiss watch brand still owned by its founding families. For nearly 150 years, the descendants of Jules Audemars and Edward Piguet have maintained independence—and created some of the most coveted watches on Earth.

Audemars Piguet occupies a rarefied position in watchmaking. Alongside Patek Philippe and Vacheron Constantin, AP forms the "Holy Trinity" of Swiss haute horlogerie. But while those brands are now owned by corporations, Audemars Piguet remains in family hands—a distinction that shapes everything from production volumes to design philosophy.

Two Friends in the Vallée de Joux

The story begins in 1875 in Le Brassus, a small village in the Vallée de Joux—the remote Swiss valley that has been the heartland of complicated watchmaking for centuries. Jules Louis Audemars, age 24, and Edward Auguste Piguet, age 22, were childhood friends who shared a passion for mechanical complexity.

Both came from watchmaking families and had trained as craftsmen specializing in complicated movements. In 1875, they formalized their partnership: Audemars would handle production and technical development, while Piguet focused on sales and external relations.

From the beginning, their ambition was clear: create the most complicated watches possible. While other manufacturers focused on volume production, Audemars Piguet dedicated itself to mechanical artistry—minute repeaters, perpetual calendars, chronographs, and combinations of all three.

"To break the rules, you must first master them." — Audemars Piguet motto

Early Masterpieces

The young company quickly established its reputation for complicated watches. In 1882, they created their first grand complication—a watch combining perpetual calendar, minute repeater, and chronograph. In 1889, they presented a grand complication at the Paris World's Fair, winning acclaim from the global horological community.

By 1892, Audemars Piguet had produced the world's first minute repeating wristwatch—decades before wristwatches became common. This wasn't just innovation for its own sake; it demonstrated that the most complex mechanisms could be miniaturized to wrist scale.

Complications as Identity

While competitors measured success by volume, Audemars Piguet measured it by complexity. The company became the go-to manufacture for grand complications, supplying movements to prestigious names including Tiffany & Co. and Cartier. This established AP's identity as a maker of exceptional pieces rather than everyday watches.

The Royal Oak Revolution

A Watch Born Overnight

By the early 1970s, the quartz crisis was devastating the Swiss watch industry. Audemars Piguet's managing director, Georges Golay, asked legendary designer Gérald Genta to create something completely new—a luxury steel sports watch that could compete with the emerging quartz threat.

The story goes that Genta designed the Royal Oak in a single night, the evening before the 1971 Basel Fair. Whether legend or fact, the result was revolutionary: an octagonal bezel with exposed screws, inspired by diving helmets; an integrated bracelet that flowed seamlessly from the case; and, most shockingly, a steel case priced higher than gold watches.

Breaking Every Rule

The Royal Oak broke every convention of luxury watchmaking:

• Steel over gold: Luxury watches were supposed to be precious metal. The Royal Oak made steel desirable—and charged gold prices for it.

• Exposed screws: Fine watches hid their construction. The Royal Oak celebrated it with hexagonal screws on the bezel.

• Integrated bracelet: Watch cases and bracelets were separate elements. The Royal Oak merged them into a unified design.

• Thin sports watch: At just 7mm thick (versus 12-14mm for typical sports watches), the Royal Oak was a dress watch with sports capability.

The "Jumbo" Reference 15202

The original Royal Oak, reference 5402 (nicknamed "Jumbo" for its then-large 39mm size), was produced until 2012 when it was replaced by the 15202. This reference remains the purest expression of Genta's design—the closest thing to the 1972 original. It commands enormous premiums on the secondary market and multi-year waitlists at retail.

The Offshore: 1993

Twenty years after the original, designer Emmanuel Gueit created the Royal Oak Offshore—a larger, bolder interpretation that amplified every element of Genta's design. At 42mm (later growing to 44mm and beyond), with pushers and crowns protected by prominent guards, the Offshore was industrial-strength luxury.

Critics called it too big, too aggressive, too much. Collectors disagreed, and the Offshore became a cultural phenomenon, particularly among athletes, musicians, and celebrities who appreciated its unapologetic presence.

Staying Independent

While other independent brands were acquired by conglomerates—Vacheron Constantin by Richemont, Jaeger-LeCoultre by Richemont, Omega by Swatch Group—Audemars Piguet resisted. The founding families retained ownership, allowing AP to make decisions based on generations rather than quarterly earnings.

This independence has practical consequences:

• Production limits: AP produces roughly 40,000 watches annually—a fraction of larger competitors. Scarcity is deliberate, not accidental.

• Single manufacture: All AP watches are produced in Le Brassus, maintaining quality control and heritage connection.

• Long-term thinking: Without shareholders demanding growth, AP can prioritize reputation over volume.

"We don't answer to Wall Street. We answer to seven generations of family legacy." — Audemars Piguet executive

Modern Collections

Royal Oak: The icon. Available from 34mm to 41mm in steel, gold, and two-tone configurations. The "Jumbo" extra-thin (15202/16202) remains the grail; the 15500 is the current mainstream reference.

Royal Oak Offshore: The bold sibling. Larger cases (42mm-44mm), prominent crown guards, and often bold colors or materials. Popular among those who find the standard Royal Oak too refined.

Code 11.59: AP's 2019 attempt to create a new icon. Mixed initial reception has given way to growing appreciation for its complex case construction and contemporary aesthetic.

Royal Oak Concept: Experimental pieces showcasing AP's technical capabilities. Often featuring exposed movements, unconventional materials, and prices in the six figures.

The Market Reality

Audemars Piguet watches, particularly Royal Oaks, trade at significant premiums on the secondary market. Steel Royal Oaks with retail prices around $25,000 regularly sell for $35,000-$50,000 on the grey market. The "Jumbo" references command even higher premiums.

Acquiring a Royal Oak at retail typically requires an existing relationship with an authorized dealer, often demonstrated by previous purchases. The waitlist system has become its own subculture, with collectors strategizing about how to establish themselves as "good customers."

This scarcity is partly genuine (limited production) and partly manufactured (distribution controls), but the result is undeniable: Royal Oak ownership has become a status symbol within the watch collecting community itself.

The Legacy

Audemars Piguet's achievement goes beyond creating desirable watches. The brand proved that steel could be luxurious, that sports watches could be refined, and that breaking conventions could define new standards.

The Royal Oak spawned an entire category—the luxury steel sports watch—that now includes the Patek Philippe Nautilus, Vacheron Constantin Overseas, and dozens of homages and competitors. Every integrated-bracelet sports watch produced today owes something to Gérald Genta's overnight sketch.

But perhaps AP's greatest achievement is survival itself. In an industry dominated by conglomerates, a family-owned company in a remote Swiss valley still produces some of the world's most coveted watches. Jules Audemars and Edward Piguet would recognize their company today—and that continuity, in an age of disruption, may be the most impressive complication of all.
