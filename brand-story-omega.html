The History of Omega

January 2, 2025 · 13 min read

No watch brand can claim a more extraordinary journey than Omega. From timing Olympic Games to accompanying astronauts to the Moon, Omega has been present at humanity's most defining moments for over 175 years.

The Greek letter Ω (Omega) symbolizes perfection and achievement—the ultimate accomplishment. It's a fitting name for a brand that has consistently pushed the boundaries of what a timepiece can endure and achieve.

Humble Beginnings

In 1848, 23-year-old Louis Brandt opened a small watch assembly workshop in La Chaux-de-Fonds, Switzerland. Working from his home, Brandt assembled key-wound precision pocket watches from parts supplied by local craftsmen. He sold them across Europe, traveling from country to country with his wares.

After Brandt's death in 1879, his sons Louis-Paul and César took over the business and transformed it. They moved operations to Biel/Bienne in 1880 and began manufacturing watches industrially—a revolutionary approach at a time when watchmaking was largely a cottage industry of independent artisans.

The Birth of Omega

In 1894, the Brandt brothers developed a groundbreaking caliber that would change the company forever. The 19-ligne "Omega" movement was revolutionary in its precision and could be repaired by simply replacing a part—no skilled adjustment required. This was unheard of in an era when every watch repair demanded a master craftsman.

The movement was so significant that in 1903, the company renamed itself "Omega" after their most famous creation. The symbol Ω became their trademark, representing the ultimate achievement in watchmaking.

"Omega" in Greek means "the end" or "the ultimate"—the last letter of the alphabet representing the highest achievement. The Brandt brothers chose it to signify their caliber represented the pinnacle of horological development.

Timing the World

Olympic Heritage

Omega's relationship with the Olympic Games began in 1932 at the Los Angeles Games, where the brand served as official timekeeper for the first time. A single technician arrived with 30 chronographs to time every event—a far cry from today's sophisticated systems involving hundreds of specialized devices.

Since then, Omega has served as Olympic timekeeper 31 times, developing increasingly precise technologies: photo-finish cameras, touchpads for swimming, false-start detection systems, and timing accurate to one millionth of a second.

Olympic Timing Milestones

1948 London: First photo-finish camera
1968 Mexico City: First touchpads for swimming
2012 London: Quantum Timer accurate to 1 millionth of a second
2024 Paris: Motion sensing technology for gymnastics

The Space Race

Qualifying for NASA

In 1962, astronaut Wally Schirra wore his personal Omega Speedmaster during the Mercury-Atlas 8 mission. NASA took notice and began searching for an official crew watch. The space agency subjected multiple brands to brutal testing: extreme temperatures (-18°C to +93°C), humidity, shock, vibration, acceleration, and vacuum.

Only the Speedmaster survived all tests. In 1965, NASA officially certified it as "Flight Qualified for all Manned Space Missions." It remains the only watch ever to receive this certification.

First Watch on the Moon

On July 21, 1969, Neil Armstrong stepped onto the lunar surface wearing an Omega Speedmaster. (His wasn't actually worn during the moonwalk—it was left inside as backup for the Lunar Module's electronic timer. Buzz Aldrin's Speedmaster became the first watch worn on the Moon.)

But the Speedmaster's finest hour came during Apollo 13. When an oxygen tank exploded and crippled the spacecraft, astronauts used their Speedmasters to time the critical 14-second engine burn that corrected their trajectory and brought them home safely. The mission's failure became NASA's "successful failure," and the Speedmaster proved its worth far beyond telling time.

The James Bond Connection

In 1995, Omega replaced Rolex as James Bond's watch of choice when Pierce Brosnan wore a Seamaster in "GoldenEye." The partnership has continued through every Bond film since, with the Seamaster becoming as integral to 007's image as his Aston Martin.

The relationship isn't just product placement—Omega creates limited edition Bond watches and has developed special features inspired by the films, including watches with hidden compartments and unusual functions.

Technical Innovation

The Co-Axial Escapement

In 1999, Omega made watchmaking history by commercializing the Co-Axial escapement—the first practical new escapement in 250 years. Invented by English watchmaker George Daniels, the system dramatically reduces friction, extending service intervals and improving long-term accuracy.

Today, virtually every mechanical Omega uses an updated Co-Axial movement, giving the brand a genuine technical differentiator rather than just marketing claims.

Master Chronometer Certification

In 2015, Omega partnered with METAS (the Swiss Federal Institute of Metrology) to create the industry's most stringent certification standard. Master Chronometer watches must pass eight separate tests, including exposure to magnetic fields of 15,000 gauss—strong enough to affect a watch pressed directly against an MRI machine.

This isn't just a marketing exercise: Master Chronometer certification requires testing of actual finished watches, not just movements. It represents the most rigorous quality standard in the industry.

Iconic Collections

Speedmaster

The "Moonwatch" remains largely faithful to its 1957 design. The hand-wound Speedmaster Professional is still produced today with hesalite crystal and the manual-wind caliber, offering the closest experience to what astronauts wore to the Moon.

Seamaster

Born in 1948 for Omega's centennial, the Seamaster has evolved from elegant dress watch to serious dive instrument. The current lineup includes the 300M (Bond's watch), Planet Ocean (600M), and Ultra Deep (6,000M).

Constellation

Omega's dress watch line, distinguished by its "claws" gripping the crystal and star emblem on the dial. The Constellation has represented refined luxury since 1952.

De Ville

Classic elegance for those who prefer understated sophistication. The De Ville Trésor offers slim, dress-watch aesthetics with Master Chronometer performance.

The Modern Era

Today, Omega operates as part of the Swatch Group, the world's largest watch conglomerate. But rather than dilute the brand, this relationship has provided resources for technical development that few independent brands could match.

Omega continues to innovate: the Seamaster Ultra Deep tested to 15,000 meters, silicon balance springs resistant to magnetism and temperature changes, and ceramic materials that eliminate scratching. The brand that went to the Moon in 1969 is still pushing boundaries today.

The Legacy

Omega's story is unique in watchmaking. No other brand can claim to have timed the Olympics, gone to the Moon, and graced the wrist of James Bond. It's a heritage of precision, adventure, and glamour that few companies in any industry can match.

But beyond the famous associations, Omega represents something more fundamental: the belief that a watch should do more than tell time. It should be precise enough to time Olympic champions, reliable enough to navigate astronauts home, and elegant enough for the world's most famous spy. That's the Omega promise—and they've been delivering it for 175 years.
