The History of Girard-Perregaux

January 2025 · 9 min read

Master of the Tourbillon with Three Gold Bridges. Since 1791, Girard-Perregaux has created architectural masterpieces in watchmaking, combining technical excellence with artistic vision.

Jean-François Bautte's Foundation

In 1791, Jean-François Bautte established a watchmaking workshop in Geneva. A prodigy who created his first watch at age 12, Bautte developed new manufacturing techniques that increased production quality while maintaining artisanal standards.

His innovations in division of labor and specialization would influence Swiss watchmaking for generations. By the time of his death in 1837, Bautte had built one of Geneva's most respected watch manufactures.

The Girard-Perregaux Union

In 1856, Constant Girard married Marie Perregaux, and together they established Girard-Perregaux in La Chaux-de-Fonds. Constant Girard was a brilliant watchmaker; Marie Perregaux brought business acumen and connections. Their partnership would elevate the brand to international prominence.

Under Constant Girard's direction, the manufacture won numerous prizes at international exhibitions and supplied timepieces to naval officers worldwide. The company became known for marine chronometers and precision instruments.

The Three Gold Bridges

In 1867, Constant Girard created what would become the brand's signature: the Tourbillon with Three Gold Bridges. The movement's bridges—which hold the barrel, center wheel, and tourbillon—were formed into arrow shapes and finished in gold.

The design won a gold medal at the Paris Universal Exhibition and has remained GP's most iconic creation. The three parallel bridges are now instantly recognizable, appearing across the brand's contemporary collection in various interpretations.

Key Collections

  • Laureato: The 1975 luxury sports watch icon
  • Bridges: Architectural movement masterpieces
  • 1966: Classical dress watch elegance
  • Vintage 1945: Art Deco-inspired tonneau
  • Cat's Eye: Dedicated ladies' collection

The Laureato Legacy

In 1975, Girard-Perregaux introduced the Laureato—a luxury sports watch with an octagonal bezel and integrated bracelet. It joined the Audemars Piguet Royal Oak (1972) and preceded the Patek Philippe Nautilus (1976) in defining the luxury steel sports watch category.

Though not as commercially successful as its competitors at the time, the Laureato has experienced a dramatic renaissance in recent years. Collectors now recognize it as an undervalued classic, driving increased demand and appreciation.

Quartz Innovation

While Japanese companies led the quartz revolution, Girard-Perregaux played a crucial role in Swiss quartz development. In the 1970s, the brand developed a high-frequency quartz movement that achieved unprecedented accuracy.

Their quartz calibers were among the finest produced in Switzerland, proving that the country could compete in electronic watchmaking while maintaining quality standards.

Manufacture Excellence

Today, Girard-Perregaux is one of the few truly integrated manufacturers, producing movements entirely in-house. Their La Chaux-de-Fonds facility handles everything from research and development to final assembly and decoration.

The brand produces its own hairsprings—a capability shared by only a handful of manufacturers worldwide. This level of vertical integration ensures complete control over quality and innovation.

Kering Era

In 2011, Kering (then PPR) acquired a majority stake in Girard-Perregaux. The luxury group's resources have supported expanded production capabilities and marketing reach while preserving the brand's independence and character.

Under Kering ownership, GP has refined its collection, focusing on the Laureato, Bridges, and 1966 families. The brand has found a clearer identity, balancing haute horlogerie complications with more accessible entry points.

Contemporary Vision

The modern Bridges collection reimagines the Three Gold Bridges concept for contemporary tastes. Sapphire cases reveal the architectural movement from all angles. Ceramic and titanium provide modern material alternatives while honoring the original vision.

The Laureato has become GP's most important commercial collection, offering everything from time-only models to skeletonized versions and chronographs. Its growing popularity suggests the market is finally recognizing what collectors have known: the Laureato deserves its place among the great luxury sports watches.

More than two centuries after Jean-François Bautte's first workshop, Girard-Perregaux continues to create watches that are as much architectural sculptures as timekeeping instruments.
