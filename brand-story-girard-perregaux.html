The History of Girard-Perregaux

January 2025 · 13 min read

"Bridging Time Since 1791." The Three Bridges tourbillon is one of watchmaking's most instantly recognizable movements—three parallel arrow-shaped bridges holding the going train in perfect alignment. This iconic design has represented Girard-Perregaux for over 150 years, symbolizing a manufacture that has survived longer than most nations.

Geneva Beginnings

The company's origins trace to 1791, when Jean-François Bautte established a watchmaking workshop in Geneva. Bautte was remarkable for his era—he created complete watches in-house at a time when most watchmakers assembled components from various suppliers. His thin watches and repeater mechanisms earned admiration throughout Europe.

Bautte died in 1837, but his enterprise continued through successors. In 1852, Constant Girard married Marie Perregaux, and the couple eventually assumed control. The hyphenated name "Girard-Perregaux" would become one of horology's most respected.

The Move to La Chaux-de-Fonds

In 1856, the company relocated to La Chaux-de-Fonds in the Neuchâtel mountains. This move proved strategically brilliant—the town was becoming a center of Swiss watchmaking, with excellent schools and a pool of skilled craftsmen. Girard-Perregaux established its manufacture here, where it remains today.

Constant Girard proved an exceptional watchmaker and businessman. Under his leadership, the company won numerous prizes at international exhibitions and established relationships with markets worldwide, including significant business in the Americas.

The Three Bridges Masterpiece

In 1867, Constant Girard created a pocket watch movement that would become the brand's signature: the tourbillon with three parallel bridges. The design was purely functional—three arrow-shaped bridges support the barrel, wheel train, and tourbillon carriage. But Girard executed them with such beauty that the movement became art.

"The bridges are not decoration—they are structure. Their beauty comes from perfect function, not ornament."
— Constant Girard

The Three Bridges tourbillon won a Gold Medal at the 1889 Paris Universal Exhibition, the same fair that debuted the Eiffel Tower. This recognition established Girard-Perregaux among the elite of Swiss watchmaking—a position it has maintained through multiple ownership changes and industry upheavals.

Three Bridges: Design Elements

  • Arrow Shape: Each bridge tapers to a point, creating visual flow
  • Parallel Alignment: Three bridges in perfect parallel formation
  • Neo-Classical Style: Gold decoration with hand-engraved patterns
  • Functional Layout: Barrel, gear train, and tourbillon each supported by one bridge
  • Visible Architecture: Movement designed to be admired from both sides

The Quartz Revolution

Ironically, the traditional manufacture played a crucial role in quartz watchmaking. In 1970, Girard-Perregaux developed a quartz movement vibrating at 32,768 Hz—a frequency that would become the industry standard still used today. Every quartz watch in the world operates at this frequency because of Girard-Perregaux's engineering.

The company successfully navigated the quartz crisis by producing both quartz and mechanical watches. This flexibility ensured survival when many competitors failed. By the 1990s, Girard-Perregaux was ready to lead the mechanical renaissance.

Three Bridges Returns

In 1991, Girard-Perregaux achieved something remarkable: adapting the legendary Three Bridges tourbillon to wristwatch size. The miniaturization required rethinking every component while preserving the visual impact that made the original famous. The result reestablished Girard-Perregaux among haute horlogerie's elite.

Modern iterations include variations with titanium bridges, rose gold finishing, and even diamond settings. But the essential design—three parallel arrow-shaped bridges supporting the movement—remains unchanged from Constant Girard's 1867 masterpiece.

The Constant Escapement

In 2011, Girard-Perregaux unveiled perhaps its most significant technical innovation: the Constant Escapement. This mechanism addresses a fundamental problem in mechanical watchmaking—the decreasing force delivered by the mainspring as it unwinds, which affects accuracy.

The Constant Escapement uses a silicon blade that buckles and releases, delivering identical impulses to the balance wheel regardless of mainspring tension. The result approaches perfect isochronism—consistent timekeeping throughout the power reserve. The innovation earned awards across the industry.

Key Collections

Bridges

The collection showcasing Girard-Perregaux's signature aesthetic. From the classical Three Bridges Tourbillon to more accessible Neo Bridges, these watches display the iconic parallel bridge architecture. Skeletonized dials reveal the mechanical artistry beneath.

Laureato

Originally launched in 1975, the Laureato features an integrated bracelet design comparable to the Royal Oak and Nautilus. The octagonal bezel and Clous de Paris dial pattern create a distinctive luxury sports watch. Recent years have seen significant collection expansion.

1966

Classical round watches with clean aesthetics, named for a significant year in company history. The 1966 collection offers refined dress watches suitable for formal occasions, available with complications including annual calendar and moon phase.

Cat's Eye

Feminine watches featuring an oval case shape inspired by gemstone cuts. These aren't simplified men's watches—they're designed specifically for women, with proportions and complications appropriate to smaller wrists.

La Chaux-de-Fonds Manufacture

Girard-Perregaux maintains one of watchmaking's most complete manufactures. The La Chaux-de-Fonds facility handles everything from movement development to case production, from hands to dials. Few competitors match this vertical integration.

The manufacture produces several base calibers that serve across collections, alongside dedicated movements for complications. This combination of standardization and specialization allows both efficiency and flexibility—essential for a mid-sized independent brand.

The Kering Era

In 2022, Girard-Perregaux and sister brand Ulysse Nardin joined Kering, the luxury group behind Gucci and Balenciaga. This partnership provides resources for continued development while maintaining brand independence. Under Kering's ownership, both brands have refreshed their collections while honoring heritage.

What Defines Girard-Perregaux

  • Three Bridges: The iconic movement architecture since 1867
  • Heritage: Continuous operation since 1791
  • 32,768 Hz: Established the quartz frequency standard
  • Constant Escapement: Revolutionary silicon-blade innovation
  • Manufacture: Vertically integrated production in La Chaux-de-Fonds
  • Laureato: Integrated bracelet sports watch since 1975

The Collector's Perspective

Girard-Perregaux occupies an interesting position in watch collecting. The brand lacks the mainstream recognition of Rolex or Omega, but commands deep respect among enthusiasts. The Three Bridges is instantly recognizable to those who know watches; the Constant Escapement impresses those who understand mechanics.

For collectors who find the usual suspects too common, Girard-Perregaux offers genuine heritage without ubiquity. The manufacture capabilities ensure quality, while the lower profile means less premium over retail than hyped competitors. It's a thinking collector's brand.

Bridging Centuries

From Jean-François Bautte's 1791 Geneva workshop to today's La Chaux-de-Fonds manufacture, Girard-Perregaux has survived longer than most institutions. The brand navigated the transition from pocket watches to wristwatches, from mechanical to quartz and back again, from family ownership to corporate structures.

Through it all, the Three Bridges endures—three parallel lines supporting the movement, just as they did when Constant Girard first designed them. In watchmaking, where some "heritage brands" trace history through acquisitions and name purchases, Girard-Perregaux offers genuine continuity: same name, same location, same iconic design, for over 230 years.
