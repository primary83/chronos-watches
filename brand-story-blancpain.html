The History of Blancpain

January 2025 · 14 min read

"Since 1735, there has never been a quartz Blancpain watch. And there never will be." This bold statement defines a manufacture that predates the United States of America and has steadfastly championed mechanical watchmaking through every crisis. Founded in the Swiss Jura village of Villeret, Blancpain is the world's oldest watch brand.

The Villeret Origins

In 1735, Jehan-Jacques Blancpain registered as a watchmaker in Villeret, establishing what would become the world's oldest continuously operating watch brand. The village, nestled in the Val-de-Ruz valley, already had a tradition of precision crafts. But Blancpain would elevate this tradition to new heights over nearly three centuries.

For generations, the Blancpain family passed watchmaking knowledge from father to son. The manufacture grew steadily, establishing a reputation for quality that attracted attention beyond the Swiss borders. By the 19th century, Blancpain had become one of Switzerland's most respected names in haute horlogerie.

The Fifty Fathoms: Birth of Modern Diving

In 1953, Blancpain's CEO Jean-Jacques Fiechter, himself a passionate diver, developed the Fifty Fathoms—widely considered the first modern dive watch. Fiechter collaborated with the French Navy's combat divers to understand their requirements: reliable timing, unidirectional rotating bezel, excellent water resistance, and visibility in murky depths.

"I was a diver before I was a watchmaker. I knew exactly what we needed underwater—and what existing watches lacked."
— Jean-Jacques Fiechter

The resulting watch was revolutionary. Its unidirectional bezel prevented accidental rotation that could lead divers to miscalculate remaining air supply. The automatic movement freed divers from winding concerns. And the name—Fifty Fathoms (approximately 91 meters)—referenced the depth achievable with period diving equipment.

Jacques Cousteau and Hollywood

The Fifty Fathoms gained global recognition through Jacques Cousteau. The legendary oceanographer wore the watch during the filming of his Academy Award-winning documentary "The Silent World" (1956). The exposure established the Fifty Fathoms as the dive watch of serious underwater explorers.

Military forces worldwide adopted the watch. The French Navy's Combat Swimmers, U.S. Navy frogmen, German Kampfschwimmers, and Israeli special forces all used Fifty Fathoms variants. Each military specified modifications—some requiring no radiation symbols, others wanting different case materials—but all recognized Blancpain's superiority underwater.

Fifty Fathoms: Design Innovations

  • Unidirectional Bezel: First implementation for safety—prevents overestimating remaining air
  • Double-Sealed Crown: Patented moisture protection system
  • Automatic Movement: No winding required in diving conditions
  • Luminous Markers: Visible in any light condition underwater
  • 91-Meter Rating: "Fifty Fathoms" referenced contemporary dive limits

The Biver Resurrection

By the early 1980s, the quartz crisis had devastated Swiss watchmaking. Blancpain, like many manufactures, struggled. Enter Jean-Claude Biver—a young marketing genius who would become one of watch industry's most influential figures. In 1983, Biver and watchmaker Jacques Piguet purchased the dormant brand for approximately $22,000.

Biver's strategy was radical: position Blancpain as the antithesis of quartz. He announced that Blancpain would never make a quartz watch—a bold statement when digital watches dominated the market. Each timepiece would be mechanical, limited in production, and crafted to the highest standards. The strategy worked brilliantly.

"In an era of mass-produced quartz watches, we chose to celebrate what machines could never replicate: human craftsmanship, tradition, and soul."
— Jean-Claude Biver

The Six Masterpieces

In 1991, Blancpain achieved something unprecedented: they announced the completion of "Six Masterpieces"—one watch for each grand complication of traditional watchmaking. These included the world's thinnest minute repeater, a tourbillon, a perpetual calendar, a split-seconds chronograph, a moonphase, and an ultra-thin automatic.

The Six Masterpieces demonstrated that haute horlogerie had survived the quartz crisis. Blancpain proved that demand existed for complicated mechanical watches at the highest levels—a lesson that would guide the industry's recovery.

Key Collections

Fifty Fathoms

The legendary dive watch collection, now available in numerous configurations from the classical Automatique to the extreme Fifty Fathoms X Fathoms with mechanical depth gauge. The collection honors its 1953 origins while incorporating modern materials and movements.

Villeret

Named for Blancpain's founding village, the Villeret collection represents classical haute horlogerie at its finest. These elegant dress watches feature grand complications—perpetual calendars, tourbillons, minute repeaters—presented with restrained aesthetics that emphasize craftsmanship over flash.

Métiers d'Art

Blancpain's artistic showcase, featuring traditional decorative crafts applied to watch dials. Enamel painting, shakudō (Japanese metalwork), Binchōtan (charcoal dial), and other rare techniques appear in strictly limited editions, often depicting natural scenes with remarkable detail.

Ladybird

Originally launched in 1956, the Ladybird was one of the first round mechanical watches designed specifically for women. The modern collection continues this legacy with sophisticated complications in feminine proportions.

Ocean Commitment

Blancpain has leveraged its diving heritage for ocean conservation. The "Blancpain Ocean Commitment" supports marine research expeditions, conservation efforts, and underwater photography. Limited edition Fifty Fathoms models fund specific projects, connecting purchasers directly to environmental impact.

This isn't mere marketing—Blancpain has funded dozens of scientific expeditions, helped establish marine protected areas, and supported breakthrough research on pristine reef ecosystems. The brand's connection to the ocean runs deeper than product positioning.

The Villeret Manufacture

In 2007, Blancpain returned home. The brand inaugurated a new manufacture in Villeret, just steps from where Jehan-Jacques Blancpain registered as a watchmaker in 1735. The building combines traditional architecture with cutting-edge production facilities, symbolizing Blancpain's balance of heritage and innovation.

Within the manufacture, master watchmakers create everything from simple three-hand movements to grande complications. The vertical integration allows Blancpain to control quality at every stage—from hairspring production to final assembly. Few manufactures match this level of self-sufficiency.

What Defines Blancpain

  • Founded 1735: The world's oldest watch brand
  • No Quartz Ever: Committed exclusively to mechanical watchmaking
  • Fifty Fathoms: Pioneer of the modern dive watch
  • Six Masterpieces: Proved haute horlogerie survived quartz
  • Ocean Commitment: Active marine conservation efforts
  • Villeret Roots: Returned to founding village

The Legacy Continues

Nearly three centuries after Jehan-Jacques Blancpain picked up his first watchmaking tools, the manufacture he founded continues to innovate. The commitment to mechanical watchmaking—radical when Biver announced it in 1983—now seems prescient. As digital devices increasingly dominate our lives, the appeal of handcrafted mechanical watches has only grown.

For collectors who value history, the Blancpain provenance is unmatched. For divers, the Fifty Fathoms remains a benchmark. For connoisseurs of haute horlogerie, the Villeret complications represent Swiss craftsmanship at its finest. And for anyone who appreciates principled business, Blancpain's refusal to compromise its mechanical heritage offers inspiration.

In a world that often prizes novelty over tradition, Blancpain proves that some things are worth preserving—and that the oldest way can sometimes be the best way.
