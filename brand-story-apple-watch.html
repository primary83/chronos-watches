The History of Apple Watch

January 2025 · 10 min read

Apple Watch didn't just enter the watch market—it redefined what a watch could be. Since 2015, it has become the world's best-selling watch, period, while pioneering health features that literally save lives.

The Announcement

Tim Cook unveiled Apple Watch in September 2014, calling it "the most personal device we've ever created." The watch industry largely scoffed. Swiss executives dismissed it as a gadget, not a real watch. They would soon regret that arrogance.

April 2015 Launch

The original Apple Watch arrived in three tiers: Sport (aluminum), standard (stainless steel), and Edition (18k gold, up to $17,000). Early reviews were mixed—battery life concerns, slow apps, unclear purpose. But Apple had planted a seed.

Finding Its Purpose

Early Apple Watch tried to do everything: notifications, apps, communication, fitness. Over time, Apple refined focus toward health and fitness—the features users actually valued. This pivot transformed a gadget into something genuinely useful.

Series 2: GPS

Series 2 added built-in GPS and improved water resistance, enabling true fitness independence. Runners no longer needed phones. Swimmers could track laps. Apple Watch became a legitimate sports watch competing with Garmin.

Series 3: Cellular

Cellular connectivity allowed Apple Watch to operate independently—calls, messages, streaming music without iPhone nearby. The red dot crown became recognizable symbol of cellular capability.

Series 4: Health Revolution

Series 4 marked Apple Watch's transformation into medical device. FDA-cleared ECG detection could identify atrial fibrillation. Fall detection automatically contacted emergency services. These weren't gimmicks—they saved lives, documented in countless news stories of users alerted to heart conditions they didn't know existed.

Continued Health Innovation

Subsequent generations added blood oxygen monitoring, temperature sensing, crash detection, and increasingly sophisticated heart monitoring. Apple Watch became the most capable health monitoring device on most people's wrists.

Market Domination

Apple Watch outsells the entire Swiss watch industry combined. Not just Rolex or Omega—all Swiss brands together sell fewer units than Apple Watch alone. This represents fundamental market shift that traditional watchmakers still struggle to address.

Apple Watch Ultra

The Ultra, launched in 2022, targeted adventure and endurance athletes—Garmin's territory. Larger case, longer battery, enhanced GPS, diving capability. Apple signaled serious intent to compete in serious sports watches.

The Traditional Watch Response

Swiss brands initially ignored Apple Watch, then dismissed it, then finally acknowledged it. Some attempted smartwatch hybrids; most doubled down on craftsmanship and heritage. The market bifurcated: technology versus tradition.

Apple Watch Today

For millions, Apple Watch is simply "watch"—they've never worn traditional timepieces and see no reason to. For traditional watch enthusiasts, it's a different category entirely. Both perspectives have merit; both markets thrive.
