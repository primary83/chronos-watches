Watch Care

How to Maintain Your Luxury Watch

January 2025 • 11 min read

A luxury watch is built to last generations—but only with proper care. Whether you own a Rolex, Omega, Patek Philippe, or any fine timepiece, understanding how to maintain your watch ensures it performs flawlessly and retains its value for years to come.

Share

This guide covers everything from daily habits to professional servicing, helping you protect your investment and enjoy your watch for a lifetime.

"A watch is a delicate instrument. Treat it with respect, and it will reward you with decades of faithful service."

Daily Care Habits

The most important maintenance happens every day. Simple habits prevent most common issues.

Winding Your Watch

If you wear your automatic watch daily, your wrist movement keeps it wound. If it stops, wind it gently—about 30-40 turns of the crown should provide full power reserve. Never force the crown; if you feel resistance, stop. For manual watches, wind until you feel gentle resistance, then stop immediately.

Setting the Time

Always pull the crown out fully before adjusting the time. Move hands clockwise (unless your watch specifically allows counterclockwise adjustment). Avoid setting the date between 9 PM and 3 AM—this is when the date-change mechanism is engaged, and forcing it can cause damage.

Wearing & Removing

Put on and remove your watch over a soft surface (bed, carpet, towel) in case it slips. Fasten the bracelet or strap securely—not too tight, not too loose. You should be able to fit a finger between your wrist and the band.

What to Avoid

✓ Do

  • Store in a cool, dry place
  • Wipe down after wearing
  • Ensure crown is pushed in/screwed down
  • Service regularly
  • Keep documentation safe
  • Insure valuable pieces

✗ Don't

  • Expose to extreme temperatures
  • Wear near strong magnets
  • Use chemicals (perfume, cleaners)
  • Ignore water resistance limits
  • Attempt DIY repairs
  • Store on speakers or electronics

Temperature Extremes

Extreme heat or cold affects lubricants and can cause the movement to run fast or slow. Don't leave your watch in a hot car, near heating vents, or exposed to direct sunlight for extended periods. Similarly, extreme cold can thicken lubricants and affect accuracy.

Magnetic Fields

Magnetism is a watch's invisible enemy. Smartphones, tablets, magnetic clasps, speakers, and MRI machines can magnetize your watch, causing it to run fast—sometimes by minutes per day. Keep your watch away from electronics when not wearing it.

💡 Is Your Watch Magnetized?

If your watch suddenly starts running significantly fast (more than 30 seconds per day), it may be magnetized. A watchmaker can demagnetize it in seconds with a simple tool. Some modern watches (like Omega's Master Chronometer line) are resistant to magnetic fields up to 15,000 gauss.

Water & Moisture

Even if your watch is water-resistant, the gaskets that provide that protection degrade over time. Have water resistance tested annually if you swim or shower with your watch. Never operate the crown or pushers when the watch is wet. After saltwater exposure, rinse with fresh water.

Chemicals

Avoid contact with perfume, cologne, lotions, solvents, and cleaning products. These can damage gaskets, discolor dials, and deteriorate leather straps. Put your watch on last when getting ready, after applying any products.

Cleaning Your Watch

Regular Cleaning (Weekly)

Use a soft, lint-free cloth to wipe down your watch after wearing. This removes oils, sweat, and dust that accumulate during daily wear. A microfiber cloth works perfectly.

Deep Cleaning (Monthly)

For metal bracelets on water-resistant watches:

  • Ensure the crown is screwed down or pushed in fully
  • Use lukewarm water with a tiny drop of mild soap
  • Gently scrub with a soft toothbrush, especially between links
  • Rinse thoroughly with clean water
  • Dry completely with a soft cloth

⚠️ Never Clean Leather Straps with Water

Water damages leather. For leather straps, use a dry cloth only. If the strap becomes soiled, consider professional cleaning or replacement. Leather straps are consumable items and should be replaced every 1-2 years with regular wear.

Proper Storage

When Not Wearing

Store your watch in a dedicated watch box or pouch, away from direct sunlight and humidity. The original box works perfectly. Avoid storing watches face-down, which can scratch the crystal.

Watch Winders

Automatic watches that aren't worn regularly can be kept on watch winders, which simulate wrist movement. However, opinions differ on their necessity:

  • Pros: Keeps watch running, convenient for watches with complex date functions
  • Cons: Adds wear to the movement, unnecessary for most watches

Most experts agree that letting a watch stop occasionally is not harmful. Modern lubricants don't "settle" or "dry out" the way old oils did. Use a winder for convenience, not necessity.

Long-Term Storage

If storing a watch for months or years:

  • Have it serviced first if it's due
  • Let automatic watches wind down naturally
  • Store in a climate-controlled environment
  • Include silica gel packets to absorb moisture
  • Check periodically and wind briefly to circulate lubricants

Professional Servicing

Like a car, a mechanical watch needs periodic professional servicing. During a service, the watch is completely disassembled, cleaned, lubricated, adjusted, and reassembled. Worn parts are replaced, and water resistance is tested.

When to Service Early

Don't wait for scheduled service if you notice:

  • Significant accuracy changes (gaining or losing more than 10 seconds/day)
  • Moisture inside the crystal
  • Crown that feels loose or doesn't screw down properly
  • Hands that stick or skip
  • Power reserve that's noticeably shorter than before

Where to Service

Authorized Service Centers are generally recommended, especially for watches under warranty. They use genuine parts and have brand-trained watchmakers. However, independent watchmakers can be excellent alternatives for out-of-warranty pieces, often at lower cost.

💡 Keep Service Records

Document every service with receipts and records. This history adds value when selling and helps future owners (or your heirs) understand the watch's maintenance history. Store records with the watch's original documentation.

Protecting Your Investment

Insurance

Standard homeowner's insurance often has limits on jewelry coverage and may not cover loss. Consider:

  • A scheduled personal property rider on your existing policy
  • Dedicated jewelry insurance (Jewelers Mutual, Hodinkee Insurance, etc.)
  • Ensuring coverage includes theft, loss, and damage

Documentation

Keep all original documentation safe:

  • Original box and packaging
  • Warranty card and certificate
  • Receipt or proof of purchase
  • Service records
  • Photographs of the watch

Complete documentation can add 10-20% to resale value and is essential for insurance claims.

Photographs

Take detailed photos of your watch:

  • Front, back, and sides
  • Serial number location
  • Any unique characteristics or marks
  • With bracelet or strap

Store photos separately from the watch (cloud storage works well). Update photos after any service or modification.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Over-Polishing

Each polish removes a tiny layer of metal. Over years, excessive polishing can round edges and alter the case shape. Some collectors prefer unpolished watches that show honest wear. If you do polish, use authorized service centers that understand proper technique.

DIY Repairs

Modern watches are precision instruments with hundreds of tiny parts. Opening a case without proper tools can damage gaskets (compromising water resistance) and introduce dust. Even battery changes on quartz watches should be done by professionals to maintain water resistance.

Ignoring Problems

Small issues become big problems. Water damage spreads. Worn parts damage other parts. A $200 fix can become a $2,000 repair if ignored. When something seems wrong, get it checked.

Final Thoughts

Maintaining a luxury watch isn't complicated—it's mostly about developing good habits and respecting the craftsmanship that went into creating it. A well-maintained watch can last for generations, gaining character and history with each passing year.

The effort you put into caring for your watch today ensures that it will continue to mark life's moments—yours, and perhaps your children's and grandchildren's—for decades to come.

That's not just maintenance. That's building a legacy.
