The History of Rado

January 2025 · 9 min read

Rado transformed watchmaking by introducing materials no one thought possible in timepieces. From the first scratch-proof watch to high-tech plasma ceramics, Rado has consistently pioneered materials that other brands only later adopted.

Lengnau Beginnings

Rado was founded in 1917 as Schlup & Co. in Lengnau, Switzerland, by brothers Fritz, Ernst, and Werner Schlup. Initially producing watch movements for other brands, the company didn't adopt the Rado name until 1957, when they began marketing complete watches.

The name "Rado" was chosen for its international appeal—easy to pronounce in any language and memorable across cultures.

The DiaStar: World's First Scratch-Proof Watch

In 1962, Rado revolutionized the industry with the DiaStar—the world's first scratch-proof watch. Using hardmetal (tungsten carbide) for the case and synthetic sapphire crystal, the DiaStar resisted scratches that would mar conventional steel watches within days of wear.

The oval-shaped DiaStar became instantly recognizable and remained in production for decades. Its introduction established Rado's identity as a materials innovator, a position the brand has never relinquished.

High-Tech Ceramic Pioneer

In 1986, Rado introduced high-tech ceramic to watchmaking with the Integral collection. This wasn't decorative ceramic like pottery—it was aerospace-grade material, incredibly hard, lightweight, hypoallergenic, and permanently colored throughout (not just surface coating).

Ceramic offered advantages over metal: it wouldn't scratch under normal wear, maintained its color indefinitely, felt warm on the skin, and was lighter than steel. Rado's ceramic expertise has since been adopted by numerous luxury brands, but Rado pioneered it.

Ceramos and Material Evolution

Rado continued innovating with Ceramos—a combination of ceramic and metal alloy that offered the best properties of both materials. They developed ceramic in colors beyond black and white, eventually achieving the technically challenging feat of producing colored ceramics consistently.

Plasma high-tech ceramic, another Rado innovation, uses special gas treatment to give ceramic a metallic appearance while maintaining ceramic's scratch resistance. This created watches that looked like metal but wore like ceramic.

The True Collection

The True collection represents Rado's design philosophy distilled to its essence. Clean lines, monochromatic color schemes, and cases made entirely from high-tech ceramic create watches of striking minimalism. Collaborations with designers and architects have produced limited editions that are collected as much for their design merit as their horological qualities.

Swatch Group Membership

As part of the Swatch Group, Rado benefits from shared research and development while maintaining its focus on materials innovation. The group's resources have enabled continued development of new ceramic formulations and manufacturing techniques.

Design Partnerships

Rado has collaborated with renowned designers and architects, including Jasper Morrison, Konstantin Grcic, and the students of prestigious design schools. These partnerships have produced watches that blur the line between timepiece and industrial design object.

Captain Cook Revival

The Captain Cook collection, originally introduced in 1962 alongside the DiaStar, was revived in 2017. These vintage-inspired dive watches showed that Rado could honor heritage while incorporating modern materials. The Captain Cook proved Rado wasn't only about futuristic designs.

Rado Today

Modern Rado continues to push materials boundaries. They remain the undisputed leader in ceramic watchmaking, with expertise no competitor has fully matched. For buyers who want something genuinely different—watches that feel different, wear different, and age differently than conventional timepieces—Rado offers a unique proposition in Swiss watchmaking.
