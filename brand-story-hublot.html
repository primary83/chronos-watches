The History of Hublot

January 2, 2025 · 11 min read

"The Art of Fusion." Hublot's philosophy of combining unexpected materials has made it one of the most polarizing and successful watch brands of the 21st century. Love it or hate it, Hublot changed what luxury watches could be.

In an industry defined by tradition and heritage, Hublot is the newcomer that crashed the party. Founded in 1980—centuries after most prestigious Swiss brands—Hublot has become a billion-dollar powerhouse through audacity, innovative materials, and marketing genius. This is how it happened.

The Italian Disruption

In 1980, Italian entrepreneur Carlo Crocco did something unthinkable: he combined a gold watch case with a rubber strap. To the traditional Swiss watch industry, this was heresy. Rubber was for dive watches, for tools, for sport—not for luxury. Gold and rubber simply didn't mix.

Crocco disagreed. He believed rubber offered superior comfort, durability, and a modern aesthetic that leather couldn't match. He named his creation "Hublot"—French for "porthole"—after the distinctive screws on the bezel that resembled those on ship windows.

The Swiss establishment rejected him. No major retailer would stock the watches. The industry press dismissed them. But Crocco persisted, selling directly to customers who appreciated the unconventional combination.

"Be first, be unique, be different. If you don't do something different, you'll never be noticed." — Jean-Claude Biver

The Biver Revolution

A Legend Arrives

In 2004, Jean-Claude Biver—the legendary executive who had saved Blancpain and transformed Omega—took control of Hublot. What followed was one of the most dramatic turnarounds in watch industry history.

Biver understood that Hublot's outsider status wasn't a weakness—it was freedom. Without centuries of heritage constraining him, Biver could take risks that established brands couldn't.

The Big Bang: 2005

Biver's first major creation was the Big Bang—a 44mm chronograph that combined ceramic, titanium, carbon fiber, and rubber in a single watch. It was bold, aggressive, and completely unlike anything from traditional Swiss brands.

The watch world was divided. Traditionalists called it vulgar, oversized, and a departure from true horology. But customers loved it. The Big Bang became a phenomenon, establishing Hublot as a force in luxury watchmaking almost overnight.

The Big Bang Formula

The Big Bang's success came from a specific formula: recognizable design (the porthole bezel), unusual material combinations, limited editions, and celebrity associations. Biver created scarcity and desirability through controlled distribution and high-profile partnerships. It was as much marketing innovation as product innovation.

The Art of Fusion

Hublot's philosophy of "fusion" goes beyond marketing. The brand genuinely experiments with materials in ways other watchmakers don't:

Magic Gold: Hublot developed a scratch-resistant 18k gold alloy by fusing gold with ceramic under extreme pressure. Traditional gold scratches easily; Magic Gold maintains its luster.

Sapphire cases: Hublot produces entire cases machined from synthetic sapphire crystal—transparent watches that reveal the movement from every angle.

Carbon fiber: While others use carbon fiber decoratively, Hublot developed proprietary carbon composites for structural case components.

Texalium: Aluminum-coated carbon fiber creates a distinctive silvery appearance unique to Hublot.

Concrete, denim, linen: Hublot has incorporated materials never before used in watchmaking, pushing boundaries of what a luxury watch can include.

The LVMH Era

LVMH acquired Hublot in 2008, recognizing the brand's potential for growth. The acquisition provided resources to develop in-house movements, build a dedicated manufacture, and expand the product line.

The HUB1240 "Unico" movement—an in-house chronograph with flyback function—debuted in 2012, answering critics who questioned Hublot's horological credentials. The brand now produces multiple proprietary calibers, including tourbillons and minute repeaters.

Sports and Celebrity Marketing

Hublot pioneered aggressive sports marketing in watches. The brand has partnered with FIFA (World Cup official timekeeper), Ferrari, Manchester United, the NBA, and numerous other sports properties.

Celebrity ambassadors include Usain Bolt, Pelé, Kylian Mbappé, and many others. Hublot watches appear on wrists at major sporting events worldwide, creating visibility that traditional advertising cannot match.

Critics argue this approach prioritizes marketing over watchmaking. Supporters counter that it's brought new customers to mechanical watches who might never have considered them otherwise.

Modern Collections

Big Bang: The flagship collection, available in countless material combinations from basic steel to sapphire crystal. The Big Bang Unico with in-house movement represents the current pinnacle.

Classic Fusion: Sleeker, more understated than the Big Bang. For those who appreciate Hublot's material innovation without the aggressive styling.

Spirit of Big Bang: Tonneau-shaped cases bring the Big Bang philosophy to a different form factor.

MP Collection: Experimental haute horlogerie pieces showcasing technical capability, including tourbillons and unconventional time displays.

The Polarizing Brand

No watch brand divides opinion like Hublot. Detractors criticize the bold aesthetics, aggressive marketing, and lack of centuries-old heritage. Supporters appreciate the innovation, material science, and willingness to challenge conventions.

This polarization is arguably intentional. Jean-Claude Biver once noted that he'd rather have people love or hate Hublot than be indifferent. Strong reactions create conversation; conversation creates awareness; awareness creates sales.

The Legacy

Whether you admire Hublot or not, its impact is undeniable. The brand proved that new entrants could succeed in luxury watches through innovation and marketing rather than heritage alone. It demonstrated that rubber straps belonged on luxury pieces. It showed that unusual materials could command premium prices.

Many techniques Hublot pioneered—material fusion, celebrity partnerships, limited editions, sports timing deals—have been adopted by competitors. The industry that initially rejected Carlo Crocco's rubber strap now embraces materials and marketing strategies Hublot normalized.

The Art of Fusion isn't just about combining metals and polymers. It's about fusing tradition with disruption, craftsmanship with commerce, watchmaking with marketing. Hublot did all of that, and in doing so, changed what Swiss luxury could mean in the 21st century.
