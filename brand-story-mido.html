Brand Story

The History of Mido

January 2025 · 9 min read

Mido draws inspiration from the world's greatest architectural achievements. The Sydney Opera House, the Eiffel Tower, the Colosseum—these timeless structures inform Mido's design philosophy: watches that endure beyond fashion, built to last like the monuments they celebrate.

A Name Meaning "I Measure"

Mido was founded in 1918 by George Schaeren in Le Locle, Switzerland, at the heart of the Swiss watchmaking region. The name comes from the Spanish "Yo mido," meaning "I measure"—a declaration of the brand's fundamental purpose. From the beginning, Mido focused on creating reliable, well-designed watches accessible to a broad audience.

Early Innovation

Mido established itself as an innovator early on. In the 1930s, they introduced the first water-resistant automatic watch with a crown secured by a cork gasket. This wasn't just a technical achievement—it showed Mido's commitment to practical innovation that served wearers' actual needs.

The brand's early success was built on combining Swiss quality with accessible pricing, a formula that continues to define Mido today.

Architectural Inspiration

Mido's unique design philosophy centers on architecture. Each collection draws inspiration from iconic structures, translating architectural elements into watch design. The concept isn't merely aesthetic—it reflects Mido's belief that watches, like great buildings, should be designed to stand the test of time.

This architectural connection gives Mido collections a coherent identity that sets them apart from competitors who simply follow fashion trends.

The Collections

Baroncelli: Inspired by the Renzo Piano-designed Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II in Milan, the Baroncelli embodies classical elegance. These dress watches feature clean lines and timeless proportions.

Multifort: Taking cues from the Sydney Harbour Bridge, Multifort watches combine sporty design with robust construction. The collection ranges from field watches to GMTs.

Commander: Inspired by the Eiffel Tower's structural lattice work, Commander watches feature distinctive case construction that references the iron framework of Gustave Eiffel's masterpiece.

Ocean Star: The Ocean Star dive watch collection draws from the Sydney Opera House's sculptural forms, offering serious dive capability in an elegant package.

Swatch Group Membership

Mido has been part of the Swatch Group since 1983, positioning it within the world's largest watch conglomerate alongside brands like Omega, Longines, and Tissot. This relationship provides Mido access to shared resources and movement development while maintaining its distinct identity.

Within the Swatch Group hierarchy, Mido occupies the "affordable luxury" tier—above Tissot, below Longines—offering Swiss automatic movements at competitive prices.

The Caliber 80 Advantage

Many modern Mido watches feature Caliber 80 movements with 80-hour power reserves—double the industry standard of roughly 40 hours. This practical advantage means you can leave your Mido unworn for a weekend and find it still running Monday morning.

This extended power reserve comes from the Swatch Group's Powermatic movement platform, demonstrating how group membership benefits Mido customers directly.

Value Proposition

Mido's strength lies in offering Swiss automatic watches with sapphire crystals, solid finishing, and proven movements at prices that undercut many competitors. A Mido automatic often costs less than comparable Tissot or Hamilton models while offering similar or superior specifications.

For collectors seeking their first Swiss automatic or building a diverse collection without breaking the bank, Mido provides exceptional value.

Mido Today

Mido continues to operate from Le Locle, producing watches that honor their architectural inspiration while incorporating modern technology. The brand may lack the prestige of its Swatch Group siblings, but for those who value substance over status, Mido delivers Swiss watchmaking excellence at accessible prices.
