The History of A. Lange & Söhne

January 2025 · 14 min read

"Never Stand Still." In the rolling hills of Saxony, far from Switzerland's watchmaking valleys, a uniquely German approach to haute horlogerie flourished, was destroyed, and rose again. A. Lange & Söhne represents not just exceptional watchmaking, but one of the most remarkable resurrection stories in luxury goods history.

The Saxon Tradition

German watchmaking traces its roots to the town of Glashütte, nestled in the Ore Mountains of Saxony. Unlike the Swiss tradition that emerged from multiple competing centers, German precision watchmaking concentrated in this single small town, creating a distinct school of horological thought.

Ferdinand Adolph Lange was born in Dresden in 1815 and trained under the court watchmaker Johann Christian Friedrich Gutkaes before traveling to Switzerland, France, and England to study foreign techniques. He returned to Saxony with a revolutionary idea: to establish a watchmaking industry that could rival the Swiss in quality while developing uniquely German innovations.

The Founder's Vision

In 1845, Lange received a loan from the Saxon government to establish a watch manufacture in the economically depressed town of Glashütte. His mission was partly social—to provide employment in a region suffering from the decline of silver mining—but his standards were uncompromisingly high.

"No one should ever have to bring a Lange watch in for repair out of necessity—only for routine maintenance."
— Ferdinand Adolph Lange

Lange developed innovations that remain hallmarks of German watchmaking today: the three-quarter plate (a single plate covering most of the movement, providing rigidity and a distinctive look), the screw-mounted gold chatons (settings for jewels), and dials featuring subsidiary seconds at 6 o'clock. He also insisted on the German silver (maillechort) finish that gives Lange movements their warm, frosted appearance.

The Golden Age

Under Ferdinand Lange and later his sons Richard and Emil, the manufacture became Germany's answer to Patek Philippe. Lange pocket watches were treasured by German royalty, industrialists, and intellectuals. The quality was so exceptional that Swiss watchmakers would study Lange movements to understand German techniques.

By the early 20th century, A. Lange & Söhne had established itself among the world's finest watchmakers. The German precision aesthetic—more architecturally bold than Swiss elegance, with distinctive finishing techniques—attracted collectors who appreciated an alternative to Genevan conventions.

Destruction and Darkness

On February 13, 1945, Allied bombers destroyed much of Dresden and the surrounding area. The Lange manufacture in Glashütte was reduced to rubble. What the bombs didn't destroy, the subsequent Soviet occupation did. The communist authorities expropriated the company and merged it into the state-owned VEB Glashütter Uhrenbetriebe. The Lange name was erased.

Walter Lange, the great-grandson of Ferdinand Adolph, had learned watchmaking in the family tradition but fled to West Germany. For over four decades, the name A. Lange & Söhne existed only in history books and the memories of collectors who treasured their antique pieces.

The Resurrection

On December 7, 1990—just weeks after German reunification—75-year-old Walter Lange re-registered the company name A. Lange & Söhne. With backing from IWC owner Günter Blümlein, he set out to resurrect his family's legacy.

"After 40 years, I had the chance to start again. But we wouldn't simply restart—we would create the finest watches ever to bear the Lange name."
— Walter Lange

Starting from nothing—no factory, no machinery, few trained watchmakers—Lange and Blümlein assembled a team of master craftsmen and spent four years developing an entirely new collection. In 1994, at the Dresden Palace, they unveiled four watches that would shake the watch world: the Lange 1, Saxonia, Arkade, and Tourbillon "Pour le Mérite."

The Lange 1: An Instant Icon

The Lange 1 broke every convention of watch design. Its asymmetric dial, with an oversized date display (the "outsize date") at 1 o'clock, main dial offset to the left, and subsidiary seconds and power reserve arranged in a carefully balanced composition, was revolutionary. Nothing like it had ever been created.

The Lange 1: Design Elements

  • Outsize Date: Two separate discs display date digits, readable at a glance
  • Asymmetric Dial: Deliberately off-center arrangement creates visual tension
  • Power Reserve: Displays remaining mainspring tension
  • Solid Silver Dial: Hand-engraved and finished
  • Three-Quarter Plate: Traditional German construction visible through caseback

The outsize date, inspired by the famous five-minute clock in Dresden's Semperoper opera house, became a Lange signature. The feature uses two separate discs—one for the tens digit (0-3) and one for the units (0-9)—allowing digits nearly twice the size possible with a conventional date window.

German Silver Movements

Every Lange movement is finished to extraordinary standards, but the aesthetic differs fundamentally from Swiss decoration. Instead of the gold and rhodium-plated brass found in Swiss movements, Lange uses untreated German silver (an alloy of copper, nickel, and zinc) that develops a warm golden patina over time.

The movements feature hand-engraved balance cocks, screw-mounted gold chatons, and blued steel screws that are heated (not painted) to achieve their distinctive color. Perhaps most remarkably, every Lange movement is assembled twice: first to ensure correct function, then disassembled, cleaned, and reassembled with all components decorated and adjusted to final specifications.

Key Collections

Lange 1

The flagship collection featuring the iconic asymmetric dial and outsize date. Available in numerous variations including the Lange 1 Daymatic (automatic), Lange 1 Moon Phase, Lange 1 Time Zone, and the Grand Lange 1 in larger case sizes.

Saxonia

The most classical collection, Saxonia represents pure German dress watch aesthetics. From the ultra-thin Saxonia Thin to the complex Saxonia Annual Calendar and Saxonia Outsize Date, these watches emphasize legibility and timeless design.

Zeitwerk

The revolutionary jumping digital display watch, introduced in 2009. Three separate discs display hours, minutes, and subsidiary seconds in a mechanical digital format—the most innovative display of time since the jumping hour. The Zeitwerk Minute Repeater adds acoustic complication to this radical design.

Richard Lange

Named for Ferdinand Adolph's son, this collection honors scientific precision. The Richard Lange features regulator-style display with separate subdials for hours, minutes, and seconds—a format traditionally used in observatory clocks.

1815

Named for the founder's birth year, the 1815 collection represents classical watchmaking refined to its purest form. These watches feature traditional elements: railway-track minute ring, blued steel hands, and exceptional finishing on relatively simple calibers.

Complications and Records

A. Lange & Söhne has established itself among the elite complication makers. The Grand Complication combines perpetual calendar, chronograph, and minute repeater. The Triple Split is the world's first chronograph capable of recording comparison times of up to 12 hours. The Zeitwerk family demonstrates that innovation and tradition can coexist beautifully.

What Defines A. Lange & Söhne

  • German Silver: Distinctive untreated alloy develops patina over time
  • Three-Quarter Plate: Traditional construction providing rigidity and beauty
  • Double Assembly: Every movement assembled, taken apart, and rebuilt
  • Outsize Date: Pioneered the oversized date display now widely copied
  • Hand Engraving: Every balance cock individually engraved by hand
  • Glashütte: All production in the historic Saxon town

Walter Lange's Legacy

Walter Lange passed away in 2017 at age 92, having witnessed his family's legacy not just restored but elevated to new heights. In his final years, he came to the manufacture regularly, often visiting with collectors and sharing stories of the old days. His determination to rebuild what his family had lost over four decades stands as one of the most inspiring stories in luxury goods.

Today, A. Lange & Söhne remains in Glashütte, training new generations of watchmakers in German techniques that nearly disappeared behind the Iron Curtain. The manufacture produces only a few thousand watches annually—each one a testament to the vision of Ferdinand Adolph and the persistence of Walter.

The German Alternative

For collectors who appreciate an alternative to Swiss conventions, A. Lange & Söhne offers something unique: precision with personality, tradition with innovation, and aesthetics informed by German rationalism rather than French elegance. These watches appeal to engineers and architects, to those who appreciate visible mechanics and honest materials.

In a world dominated by Swiss watchmaking, A. Lange & Söhne proves that excellence can emerge from different traditions. The Saxon approach—more technically rigorous, more visually bold, more individually crafted—offers collectors a genuine alternative at the highest level of horology.
