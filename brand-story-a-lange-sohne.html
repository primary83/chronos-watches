Brand Story

The History of A. Lange & Söhne

January 2025 · 10 min read

German precision at its finest. From Glashütte, Saxony—a brand reborn from the ashes of war. Every movement assembled twice. The watchmaker's watchmaker.

Ferdinand Adolph Lange's Vision

In 1845, Ferdinand Adolph Lange established his watchmaking workshop in Glashütte, a small Saxon mining town that had fallen on hard times. With a loan from the Saxon government, Lange brought precision watchmaking to Germany and transformed Glashütte into the center of German horology.

Trained in Dresden and having worked in Paris, London, and Switzerland, Lange combined the best of European watchmaking traditions. He introduced the metric system to watchmaking, developed new tools and techniques, and established a training program that would sustain the industry for generations.

The Lange Standard

From the beginning, Lange watches were distinguished by exceptional quality. The three-quarter plate in German silver (untreated nickel-silver alloy) became a Lange signature. Hand-engraved balance cocks, blued steel screws heated to exactly 300°C, and meticulous hand-finishing set these watches apart.

Lange pocket watches became the choice of royalty, scientists, and captains of industry. They were prized for their precision and reliability—essential qualities in an era when accurate timekeeping was crucial for navigation and industry.

War and Rebirth

World War II and the subsequent Soviet occupation devastated Glashütte. In 1948, the Communist government expropriated Lange and other watch manufacturers, merging them into a state-owned enterprise. The Lange name disappeared from dials.

After German reunification in 1990, Walter Lange—great-grandson of the founder—returned to Glashütte with a vision to restore the family legacy. On October 24, 1994, A. Lange & Söhne unveiled four watches that announced their return: the Lange 1, Arkade, Saxonia, and Tourbillon "Pour le Mérite."

The Lange 1 Revolution

The Lange 1 was revolutionary. Its asymmetric dial layout—with off-center time display, outsize date, and power reserve indicator—broke every rule of traditional German watchmaking. Yet it was unmistakably Lange, with all the hallmarks of Saxon craftsmanship.

The outsize date, inspired by the five-minute clock at the Dresden Semper Opera, became a Lange signature. Two separate discs—one for units, one for tens—create a date display with unprecedented legibility.

Key Collections

  • Lange 1: The iconic asymmetric masterpiece
  • Saxonia: Pure dress watch elegance
  • Zeitwerk: Revolutionary mechanical digital display
  • Datograph: The king of chronographs
  • 1815: Classical pocket watch heritage
  • Odysseus: Lange's first sports watch

Assembled Twice

Every A. Lange & Söhne movement is assembled twice. First, the movement is assembled to check all functions and adjust the rate. Then it is completely disassembled, cleaned, and reassembled with fresh lubricants. This painstaking process ensures perfect function and extends service intervals.

The movements are finished to an extraordinary standard. Even components that will never be seen are decorated. German silver plates develop a warm patina over time. Screws are individually blued by hand. Chatons holding jewels are gold, not brass.

The Zeitwerk Innovation

In 2009, Lange introduced the Zeitwerk—a mechanical watch with a digital display. Jumping hours and minutes are powered by a constant-force mechanism that ensures each digit change happens instantaneously. The decimal minute repeater version chimes the time in a way that matches the digital display—a first in watchmaking.

The Zeitwerk represents Lange's philosophy: innovation in service of function, always executed to the highest possible standard.

The Datograph Legacy

The Datograph, introduced in 1999, is widely considered the finest chronograph ever made. Its movement, the L951.1, features a flyback function, column wheel, and the signature outsize date. The finishing rivals anything produced by Swiss or German competitors.

Collectors prize the Datograph for its perfect proportions, legible dial layout, and the sheer beauty of its movement. It represents the pinnacle of mechanical chronograph design.

Modern Excellence

Today, under Richemont ownership since 2000, A. Lange & Söhne continues to produce around 5,500 watches annually—a tiny fraction of Swiss luxury output. Each watch is assembled by a single watchmaker who signs the movement.

The introduction of the Odysseus in 2019 marked Lange's entry into the luxury sports watch market—their first steel watch and first integrated bracelet. It proved that Lange could innovate while maintaining their exceptional standards.

From the workshops of Glashütte, A. Lange & Söhne continues to produce watches that represent the absolute pinnacle of mechanical watchmaking—German precision, perfected.
