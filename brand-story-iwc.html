The History of IWC Schaffhausen

January 2025 · 13 min read

"Engineering Dreams Since 1868." When an American watchmaker arrived in the Swiss-German border town of Schaffhausen, he brought a revolutionary idea: combine American manufacturing efficiency with Swiss craftsmanship. The result would be the International Watch Company—IWC—a manufacture that continues to embody engineering excellence over 150 years later.

An American in Switzerland

Florentine Ariosto Jones was an unlikely founder for a Swiss watch company. Born in Boston in 1841, Jones had risen to become director of the E. Howard Watch Company, one of America's finest watchmakers. But in 1868, he arrived in Switzerland with an audacious plan: establish a watch manufacture that would use advanced American production techniques with Swiss hand-finishing—and build watches specifically for the American market.

Jones chose Schaffhausen strategically. Located on the Rhine River, the town had abundant hydroelectric power from the Rhine Falls—Europe's largest waterfall—and access to both Swiss and German skilled labor. The location was deliberately distant from the traditional Swiss watchmaking centers, allowing Jones to develop his own methods without interference.

The International Watch Company

In 1868, Jones established the "International Watch Company"—signaling his ambition for global reach. The name reflected both his American origins and Swiss home, though some Swiss watchmakers viewed the newcomer with suspicion. Jones's approach was indeed revolutionary: he introduced modern machinery for component production while maintaining handcrafted finishing and assembly.

"We shall combine the excellence of American machinery with the skills of Swiss craftsmen."
— Florentine Ariosto Jones, 1868

The early years were turbulent. The American market that Jones had targeted proved difficult to penetrate, and by 1875, financial troubles forced him to sell his stake and return to America. But the company survived under new ownership, eventually finding its identity as a manufacturer of exceptionally engineered timepieces for demanding professionals.

The Portugieser: An Unlikely Icon

In 1939, two Portuguese merchants approached IWC with an unusual request: they wanted wristwatches with the precision of marine chronometers. At the time, wristwatches were generally considered less accurate than pocket watches. IWC's solution was to fit a large pocket watch movement—Caliber 74—into an oversized wristwatch case.

The resulting Portugieser was 41.5mm in diameter—enormous for the era when most wristwatches were under 35mm. The clean dial with Arabic numerals and railway-track minute ring emphasized legibility, while the thin bezel maximized dial size. Though initially produced in small numbers, the Portugieser would become one of IWC's most beloved collections decades later.

Portugieser: Design DNA

  • Oversized Case: Originally 41.5mm, now available up to 46mm
  • Pocket Watch Movement: Marine chronometer precision in wristwatch form
  • Clean Dial: Arabic numerals, railway-track minutes, minimal decoration
  • Thin Bezel: Maximizes dial real estate for legibility
  • Dress-Sport Versatility: Elegant enough for suits, substantial enough for casual

Watches for Aviators

IWC's pilot's watch heritage began in 1936 with the Special Pilot's Watch, designed for military use. But it was the legendary Big Pilot's Watch 52 T.S.C. of 1940 that established IWC's reputation among aviators. This massive 55mm observation watch was designed for Luftwaffe navigators who needed to precisely synchronize timing during missions.

The Big Pilot featured a central hacking seconds for synchronization, antimagnetic protection, and a distinctive conical crown designed to be operated with flying gloves. The oversized dial with luminous numerals could be read at a glance in dark cockpits. Though the war ended, IWC's pilot's watch expertise would define the brand for generations.

"A pilot's watch must be readable instantly, operable with gloves, and resistant to the magnetic fields in a cockpit. Function dictates form."

The Mark Series

While the Big Pilot was an observation instrument, the Mark series served as everyday pilot's watches. The Mark 11, introduced in 1948 for the British Royal Air Force, established the template: 36mm case, antimagnetic soft iron cage, excellent legibility, and extreme reliability. RAF pilots wore the Mark 11 until 1981—a testament to its durability.

Today's Pilot's Watch Mark XX continues this lineage with modern refinements: in-house movements, improved water resistance, and updated dimensions. The Mark series remains IWC's most accessible entry point into their pilot's watch collection.

Key Collections

Portugieser

The elegant flagship collection. From the pure Portugieser Automatic to the complex Portugieser Perpetual Calendar, this range demonstrates that tool watch precision can coexist with dress watch refinement. The Portugieser Yacht Club adds sportier elements for nautical enthusiasts.

Pilot's Watches

IWC's most diverse collection spans from the entry-level Mark XX to the statement-making Big Pilot. The collection includes chronographs, world timers, perpetual calendars, and tourbillons—all maintaining the aviation-inspired aesthetic of large crowns, clear dials, and robust construction.

Portofino

Named for the Italian Riviera village, Portofino represents understated elegance. These slim dress watches offer a more conventional approach to luxury—refined, subtle, and perfectly sized for formal occasions. The Portofino serves collectors who prefer discretion over statement.

Aquatimer

IWC's dive watch collection, launched in 1967, features the innovative SafeDive rotating bezel system and robust construction rated to various depths. While less famous than some competing dive watches, the Aquatimer offers distinctive design and IWC's engineering excellence underwater.

Ingenieur

Originally designed in 1955 as an antimagnetic watch for engineers working near electrical fields, the Ingenieur has evolved into a luxury sports watch. Gerald Genta redesigned it in the 1970s with an integrated bracelet, though modern versions return to more classical proportions.

Technical Innovation

IWC has consistently pushed technical boundaries. The 1985 Da Vinci Perpetual Calendar was the first to display a four-digit year, and its programming extends to 2499. The company developed proprietary materials including ceramic, titanium aluminide, and Ceratanium (a ceramic-titanium hybrid). Recent calibers feature the Pellaton winding system, invented in the 1940s and continually refined for exceptional efficiency.

What Defines IWC

  • Engineering Focus: Form follows function philosophy
  • Pilot's Heritage: Watches designed for actual aviation use
  • The Portugieser: Pocket watch precision in wristwatch form
  • Pellaton Winding: Proprietary efficient automatic system
  • Schaffhausen: Single manufacture location since 1868
  • American-Swiss DNA: Manufacturing efficiency meets craftsmanship

The Schaffhausen Manufacture

Unlike brands scattered across multiple facilities, IWC has remained in Schaffhausen since 1868. The modern manufacture, completed in 2018, combines historical buildings with cutting-edge facilities. Here, IWC produces its own movements, cases, and many components—a level of vertical integration that few competitors match.

The company maintains strong ties to the local community. Schaffhausen's coat of arms—a ram—occasionally appears on special editions, and IWC actively supports regional initiatives. This single-site concentration preserves institutional knowledge and enables the cross-pollination of ideas between departments.

The IWC Collector

IWC attracts collectors who appreciate engineering substance over marketing flash. The brand's watches tend toward larger sizes with clear, functional designs. They appeal to professionals—pilots, engineers, scientists—who value precision and legibility. The relatively accessible price points (compared to other Richemont brands) offer entry into serious Swiss watchmaking.

For those who find the "Holy Trinity" too dressy and sports watches too casual, IWC occupies a compelling middle ground: technically sophisticated, aesthetically clean, and designed for actual use rather than display.

The Enduring Legacy

What Florentine Ariosto Jones started in 1868 continues to evolve. His vision of combining manufacturing excellence with craftsmanship remains IWC's guiding principle. The company that began making watches for American markets now serves collectors worldwide, but the engineering-first philosophy hasn't changed.

From the Portugieser's maritime precision to the Big Pilot's aviation heritage, IWC watches are designed to perform. They may lack the prestige of certain Swiss names or the hype of others, but for collectors who value substance, IWC Schaffhausen offers something increasingly rare: honest watches built by engineers, for people who appreciate engineering.
