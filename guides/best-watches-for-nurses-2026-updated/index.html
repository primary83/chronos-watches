Best Watches for Nurses 2026 — Updated Infection Control Guide

March 2026 · 12 min read
Home / Guides / Best Watches for Nurses 2026 — Updated Infection Control Guide
← Back to Guides

Nurses need watches that do things most watch guides never consider: survive repeated hand-washing and sanitizer exposure, display a seconds hand for counting pulse and respiratory rates, resist infection-control concerns about wristbands harboring bacteria, and endure 12-hour shifts of physical work without becoming uncomfortable or hazardous.

This updated guide accounts for current infection control protocols and the practical realities of nursing in 2026.

Infection Control Considerations

Many hospitals have "bare below the elbows" policies that restrict or prohibit wristwatches during direct patient care. Before buying a watch for nursing, check your facility's specific policy. Options for restricted environments include: fob watches (pin to scrubs), silicone band watches (easily cleaned between patients), and carabiner clip watches (attach to badge or pocket).

For facilities that permit wristwatches, the ideal nursing watch has: a non-porous strap (silicone, rubber, or stainless steel — not leather, which absorbs moisture and bacteria), smooth surfaces with minimal crevices where bacteria can accumulate, and water resistance sufficient to withstand frequent hand-washing and alcohol-based sanitizer exposure.

Essential Features for Nurses

Seconds hand or seconds display

Counting pulse rate (beats per 15 seconds × 4) and respiratory rate requires visible seconds. A sweep seconds hand on an analog watch or a seconds display on a digital watch is mandatory — not optional.

24-hour capability

12-hour shifts spanning AM and PM make 24-hour time useful. Some nurses prefer digital watches that display 24-hour format. Others use the 12-hour analog dial and note AM/PM contextually.

Luminous dial

Night shifts in dimly lit rooms require legible time display without turning on lights that wake patients. Strong lume (luminous material) on hands and indices is essential for night-shift nurses.

Our Picks

Casio F-91W
$10–$15

The most practical nursing watch ever made. Digital display with seconds, 24-hour format, lightweight resin case that's easily cleaned, and a price so low it's essentially disposable if infection control becomes a concern. The F-91W's backlight illuminates the display for night shifts. Water resistant for hand-washing. And at $12, you can buy five of them and rotate. There's a reason this is the most-sold watch in history — it just works.

Best for: The practical nurse who wants function without fuss.

Casio G-Shock GBD-200
$100–$130

For nurses who want durability beyond the F-91W, the G-Shock GBD-200 adds 200m water resistance, shock resistance, and a slimmer profile than traditional G-Shocks. The resin case and strap are easily wiped clean between patients. Digital display with seconds, step counter (useful for tracking movement during shifts), and Bluetooth connectivity for logging. The GBD-200 is tough enough to survive anything a hospital shift throws at it.

Best for: Nurses who want G-Shock toughness in a relatively slim package.

Apple Watch SE (with silicone sport band)
$249–$299

The Apple Watch SE on a silicone Sport Band is increasingly common in healthcare settings. Timer functions (useful for medication timing), fall detection, health tracking, and the ability to receive notifications about patient alerts make it genuinely functional for nursing. The silicone band is easily cleaned. The watch face can be customized to show seconds prominently. Concern: battery life requires daily charging, which means you need a charging routine that survives shift work fatigue.

Best for: Tech-comfortable nurses who want smart features alongside timekeeping.

Seiko 5 Sports on Silicone Strap
$180–$220 (+ $20 strap)

For nurses who want a "real" watch — a mechanical automatic — the Seiko 5 Sports on an aftermarket silicone strap combines genuine watchmaking with infection-control practicality. The sweep seconds hand is ideal for counting vitals. The 100m water resistance handles sanitizer and hand-washing. Swap the stock bracelet for a smooth silicone strap (~$20 on Amazon) and you have a watch that's easy to clean, comfortable for 12-hour shifts, and has the soul of a mechanical timepiece.

Best for: Nurses who appreciate mechanical watches and want something with character.

The Nursing Watch Rule

The best nursing watch is the one your facility allows and that you can keep clean. Function beats fashion in healthcare. A $12 Casio F-91W that you can sanitize between patients is objectively better for patient safety than a $5,000 Rolex that you're afraid to douse in hand sanitizer. Check your facility's policy first, then choose accordingly.
