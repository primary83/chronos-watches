How to Care for a Mechanical Watch

January 2025 · 10 min read

A mechanical watch is a miniature engineering marvel with hundreds of tiny components working in harmony. With proper care, your mechanical timepiece can last generations and even appreciate in value.

Understanding Your Mechanical Watch

Unlike quartz watches that run on batteries, mechanical watches are powered entirely by a mainspring that stores energy when wound. This energy is released through a series of gears and regulated by an escapement—a delicate dance of physics that has remained largely unchanged for centuries.

Whether you own a manual-wind watch that requires daily winding or an automatic (self-winding) watch that winds itself through your wrist movement, the core principles of care remain the same.

Daily Care Habits

The best thing you can do for your mechanical watch is wear it regularly. Movement keeps the lubricants distributed throughout the mechanism. If you rotate between multiple watches, try to wear each one at least once a week.

When putting on or removing your watch, do so over a soft surface like a bed or carpet. This simple habit can prevent devastating damage if the watch slips from your hands.

Winding Your Watch Correctly

For manual-wind watches, wind at the same time each day—morning is ideal. Wind until you feel gentle resistance, then stop. Never force the crown past this point, as overwinding can damage the mainspring.

For automatic watches, if the watch has stopped, give it 20-30 manual winds through the crown before wearing it. This provides an initial power reserve. Your wrist movement will keep it running throughout the day.

Daily Care Checklist

  • Wind manual watches at the same time daily
  • Avoid setting the date between 9 PM and 3 AM
  • Keep away from magnets (phones, tablets, speakers)
  • Remove before physical activities or sports
  • Wipe with a soft cloth after wearing

Avoiding Magnetic Fields

Magnetism is one of the biggest threats to mechanical watch accuracy. Modern life surrounds us with magnetic fields—smartphones, tablets, laptop speakers, magnetic clasps, and even refrigerator doors can magnetize your watch.

A magnetized watch will run fast, sometimes gaining several minutes per day. If you suspect magnetization, a watchmaker can demagnetize it in seconds using a simple tool.

Temperature and Shock Protection

Extreme temperatures affect accuracy and can damage lubricants. Avoid leaving your watch in direct sunlight, in a hot car, or exposing it to sudden temperature changes. Similarly, protect your watch from shocks—remove it before golf, tennis, or any activity involving impact.

Regular Service Intervals

Even with perfect care, mechanical watches need professional servicing every 3-5 years. During service, a watchmaker disassembles the movement, cleans every component, replaces worn parts and gaskets, re-lubricates, and adjusts for accuracy.

Think of it like a car service—the oils break down over time, and small issues caught early prevent expensive repairs later. Always use authorized service centers or reputable independent watchmakers.

Storage When Not Wearing

Store your mechanical watch in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight. A watch box with soft lining is ideal. For automatic watches you don't wear often, consider a watch winder to keep them running—but choose a quality winder with appropriate turns-per-day settings for your specific movement.

If storing for extended periods, it's actually fine to let the watch stop. The lubricants won't be harmed by the watch sitting still for a few weeks or even months.
