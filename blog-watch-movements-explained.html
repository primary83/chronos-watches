Watch Movements Explained

January 2025 • 10 min read

The movement—also called the caliber—is the engine of a watch. It's what makes the hands move, the complications function, and ultimately what separates a $50 watch from a $50,000 masterpiece. Understanding movements is fundamental to appreciating and choosing luxury timepieces.

This guide breaks down the three main types of watch movements, their advantages and disadvantages, and helps you decide which is right for your lifestyle and preferences.

"A watch is not just about telling time—it's about how that time is measured and displayed."

The Three Types of Movements

Automatic (Self-Winding)

The Enthusiast's Choice

Automatic movements harness the natural motion of your wrist to wind the mainspring. A weighted rotor spins as you move, transferring energy to the mainspring and keeping the watch running. Most luxury watches today use automatic movements.

How it works: A semicircular rotor rotates with your arm's movement, winding the mainspring through a series of gears. Power reserve typically ranges from 40-80 hours, meaning you can leave the watch off for a couple of days before it stops.

Advantages

  • No battery needed
  • Smooth sweeping seconds hand
  • Traditional craftsmanship
  • Higher resale value
  • Emotional connection

Considerations

  • Less accurate than quartz
  • Requires regular service
  • Stops if unworn
  • More expensive
  • Sensitive to shocks

Manual (Hand-Wound)

The Purist's Choice

Manual movements are the oldest and most traditional type. They require daily winding by turning the crown, creating a ritual that many watch lovers cherish. Without a rotor, manual movements are often thinner and allow for more elegant case designs.

How it works: Turning the crown winds the mainspring directly. When fully wound, the spring slowly releases energy through the gear train, moving the hands. You'll feel resistance when the mainspring is fully wound—stop there to avoid damage.

Advantages

  • Thinner case possible
  • Daily ritual connection
  • Traditional experience
  • Often beautifully finished
  • No rotor noise

Considerations

  • Requires daily winding
  • Easy to forget
  • Less convenient
  • Can overwind if careless
  • Less common today

Quartz (Battery-Powered)

The Practical Choice

Quartz movements use a battery to send electrical current through a small quartz crystal, causing it to vibrate at a precise frequency (32,768 times per second). This vibration regulates the movement with exceptional accuracy.

How it works: A battery powers an integrated circuit that sends current to a quartz crystal. The crystal's vibrations are converted into regular pulses that drive a stepping motor, moving the seconds hand in distinctive one-second ticks.

Advantages

  • Exceptional accuracy
  • Low maintenance
  • Affordable
  • Always ready to wear
  • Shock resistant

Considerations

  • Requires battery changes
  • Ticking seconds hand
  • Less prestigious
  • Lower resale value
  • Less emotional appeal

Special Movement Types

Spring Drive

Developed by Seiko, Spring Drive combines mechanical and electronic technology. A mainspring provides power (like mechanical watches), but a quartz crystal regulates accuracy (like quartz watches). The result: mechanical soul with quartz precision and a perfectly smooth seconds hand.

Solar/Eco-Drive

Solar-powered quartz movements convert light into electrical energy, eliminating battery changes. Citizen's Eco-Drive can run for months on a full charge, even in darkness. Perfect for those who want quartz convenience without battery hassles.

High-Frequency Movements

Traditional mechanical movements beat at 28,800 vibrations per hour (4 Hz). High-frequency movements like Zenith's El Primero (36,000 vph) or TAG Heuer's experimental movements offer increased accuracy and smoother seconds hand motion.

💡 Which Should You Choose?

Choose Automatic if: You appreciate craftsmanship, wear watches daily, and want something with soul and resale value.

Choose Manual if: You enjoy daily rituals, prefer thinner watches, and want the purest mechanical experience.

Choose Quartz if: You prioritize accuracy, have multiple watches, or need a grab-and-go timepiece.

In-House vs. Third-Party Movements

Many watch brands use movements from specialized manufacturers like ETA or Sellita. Others—like Rolex, Patek Philippe, and Jaeger-LeCoultre—develop movements entirely in-house.

In-house movements often command premiums because they represent significant R&D investment and brand commitment. However, excellent watches exist with both in-house and third-party calibers. A well-decorated ETA movement in a quality case can outperform a poorly finished "in-house" movement.

Don't let "in-house" marketing sway you entirely—focus on overall quality, finishing, and the watch's complete package.

Caring for Your Movement

Mechanical Watches (Auto & Manual)

  • Service regularly — Every 5-7 years, have your watch serviced by an authorized service center
  • Avoid magnets — Strong magnetic fields can magnetize components and affect accuracy
  • Wind gently — Don't force the crown; stop when you feel resistance
  • Keep it running — Regular wear or a watch winder keeps lubricants distributed
  • Avoid shocks — While modern movements are robust, extreme impacts can damage components

Quartz Watches

  • Replace batteries promptly — Dead batteries can leak and damage the movement
  • Use authorized service — Proper battery replacement maintains water resistance
  • Store with crown pulled — If storing long-term, pulling the crown stops the movement and preserves battery

The Bottom Line

There's no "best" movement type—only the best choice for your needs and preferences. Mechanical watches offer tradition, craftsmanship, and emotional connection. Quartz watches offer accuracy, convenience, and practicality.

Many collectors own both, choosing their watch based on occasion, outfit, or mood. The beauty of watches is that they're personal expressions—let your movement choice reflect what matters most to you.
